Wilde Bilder

Overview

Wilde Bilder (literally “Wild Pictures”) was a dynamic movement centred in 1980s Berlin, known for its bold, expressive painting style. Artists embraced wild energy, dramatic colour, and personal themes—responding to the divided city and a desire to break with the past.

Key Themes and Ideas

Expressive, “Wild” Painting

  • Artists used thick, energetic brushwork and intense colour to show raw emotion and personal experience.
  • This was a reaction against careful, controlled art—favouring a direct, spontaneous style.
  • Works often appear vigorous, passionate, and even chaotic.

Figurative Focus and Urban Life

  • Instead of abstract shapes, painters depicted real people, nightlife, and city scenes.
  • Reflects the energy, excitement, and sometimes loneliness of Berlin’s urban environment in the 1980s.
  • Art captures both community and the experience of feeling like an outsider.

Rebellion and Identity

  • The movement broke art rules and resisted tradition, highlighting personal and artistic freedom.
  • Explored gender, outsider status, and self-assertion—especially for women artists confronting male-dominated art.
  • Personal expression and non-conformity are celebrated throughout the movement.

Contextual Information

Main Locations

  • West Berlin, Germany (especially the alternative art and club scenes of the 1980s)

Relevant Previous Movements

  • German Expressionism (emotional art with bold colour, early 20th century)
  • Neo-Expressionism (revival of expressive painting, 1970s–80s)
  • Abstract art (which many Wilde Bilder artists reacted against)

Culture and Politics

  • Berlin in the 1980s: a divided city, political tension, and a unique underground culture
  • Artistic freedom and experimenting were encouraged, with a growing women’s movement influencing work by female artists

Writers and Music

  • Heiner Müller, Elfriede Jelinek, and other German writers explored identity and society
  • Punk and New Wave music shaped the movement’s energy; German classical composers were still admired

Materials and Technology

  • Synthetic paints, new art materials
  • TV, magazines, photocopiers, and early digital graphics began to appear, though painting was the main focus

Typical Fashion

  • Bold, colourful, and punk-inspired clothing for men and women: leather jackets, graphic T-shirts, ripped jeans, and dramatic hair (dyed, spiked)
  • The look reflected the rebellious, expressive spirit of the art

Period

  • 1985–1990 (core years, with some artists starting before or after)

Key Artists

Rainer Fetting

Rainer Fetting, Berliner Mauer 1981
  • Key figure in Berlin’s “Junge Wilde” movement, known for emotionally charged, figurative painting.
  • Used bold brushwork and vivid colour to depict Berlin landscapes, the Berlin Wall, and portraits.
  • His style blends influences from Van Gogh and contemporary urban themes.
  • Works reflect both city energy and personal emotion—held in international collections.

Salomé

Salomé, Gesicht im Schatten 1986
  • Provocative German painter; co-founder of Galerie am Moritzplatz in Berlin, shaping the Neo-Expressionist scene.
  • Known for vivid colours, energetic brushwork, and exploring sexuality and urban nightlife.
  • Work often examines themes of identity and the outsider’s perspective.

KH Hödicke

KH Hödicke, Berlin 1985
  • Pioneer and mentor of the Wilde Bilder/Junge Wilde movement.
  • Paintings combine energetic brushwork, abstract and figurative elements, and urban Berlin scenes.
  • Dynamic approach bridges abstraction and figuration, capturing cultural shifts.

Helmut Middendorf

Helmut Middendorf, Night Hawk 1982
  • Known for large, expressive canvases blending abstract and figural imagery.
  • Focused on nightlife, music, and Berlin city scenes, painted with pulsing energy and colour.
  • Captures the raw excitement and spontaneity of urban life.

Elvira Bach

Elvira Bach, Selbstbildnis 1982
  • Major female figure in Wilde Bilder and “Junge Wilde.”
  • Paints striking, expressive portraits of women—focusing on power, identity, and self-assertion.
  • Bold colour and form address feminist themes and personal narrative.

Maria Lassnig

Maria Lassnig, Du oder Ich 2005
  • Austrian pioneer known for “body awareness” (Körperbewusstsein) paintings—showing how a body is felt from within.
  • Her self-portraits are expressive, often distorted, and intensely personal.
  • Important influence on feminist and contemporary figuration worldwide.

Three Short Points for Students

  • Wilde Bilder artists expressed strong emotions and experiences through wild brushwork, vivid colour, and real-life city scenes.
  • The movement captured Berlin’s energy and chaos in the 1980s while exploring themes of identity, rebellion, and being an outsider.
  • Women artists like Elvira Bach and Maria Lassnig used painting to assert individuality and challenge gender roles.

Glossary for Students

  • Expressionism: Art showing strong feelings through colour and style rather than realism.
  • Figurative: Art that represents real things, especially people, rather than pure abstraction.
  • Feminist: Relating to supporting equal rights and opportunities for women.
  • Wilde Bilder: German for “Wild Pictures”—capturing raw emotion, city life, and personal stories.
