New Expressionism and New Image Painting

Overview

New Expressionism (also called Neo-Expressionism) and New Image Painting emerged in the late 1970s, reviving intense emotion, bold colours, and figurative imagery. Artists rejected the cool, detached styles of Minimalism and Conceptual Art, choosing instead to communicate raw feeling, personal stories, and social themes through energetic painting and mixed materials.

Key Themes and Ideas

Raw Emotion, Bold Colour, and Vigorous Brushwork

  • Artworks are defined by thick, expressive brushstrokes and vibrant, sometimes clashing, colours.
  • Artists like Georg Baselitz, Anselm Kiefer, and Julian Schnabel created paintings that appear spontaneous, rough, or even violent.
  • Works often feel immediate and powerful, communicating strong feeling and personal vision.

Return to the Figure and Storytelling

  • Instead of abstraction, these artists returned to recognisable images, especially human figures, though often distorted or cartoon-like.
  • Their work tells stories or refers to history, myth, politics, or personal experiences.
  • For example, Kiefer’s paintings address German history, while Basquiat incorporated graffiti and text to reflect on identity and society.

Mixing Materials and Breaking Boundaries

  • Artists combined paint with sand, fabric, found objects, collage, and more for a textured, tactile effect.
  • The movement blurred lines between painting and sculpture, and between “high” art and popular culture, drawing inspiration from punk, Pop Art, and street art.
  • This openness led to a rebellious, experimental spirit—and also contributed to art’s commercial boom in the 1980s.

Contextual Information

Main Locations

  • Germany (Berlin, Düsseldorf)
  • United States (New York)
  • Also active in Italy, France, the UK

Influences and Previous Movements

  • German Expressionism (early 20th century focus on emotion and distortion)
  • Abstract Expressionism (emotional, gestural painting)
  • Rejection of Minimalism and Conceptual Art (seen as too intellectual or cold)
  • Influences from Pop Art and Punk (energy, attitude, imagery)

Culture and Politics

  • 1970s–1990s: Political change (Cold War, reunification), economic uncertainty, and cultural soul searching
  • Desire for personal expression, authentic engagement, and reaction against detachment of earlier art
  • 1980s art market boom made some artists celebrities and increased commercialism

Writers and Music

  • Writers: Jean-Paul Sartre (existentialism), German poets like Paul Celan (inspired Kiefer), critics like Donald Kuspit
  • Music: Punk, New Wave, and experimental composers (e.g. Philip Glass)

Technology and Materials

  • New synthetic paints, mixed media, spray paint, and photocopying expanded artistic techniques
  • Mass media, television, early computers gradually influenced both content and style

Fashion of the Era

  • Men: Leather jackets, ripped jeans, graphic T-shirts, trainers—styles from punk and streetwear
  • Women: Bold patterns, oversized pieces, power suits, dramatic makeup, and big hair
  • Both: Fashion was expressive, rebellious, mixing old and new styles

Period

  • 1975–1995 (core years for Neo-Expressionism and New Image Painting)

Key Artists

Georg Baselitz

Georg Baselitz, Die grosse Nacht im Eimer 1962–63
  • Pioneering German Neo-Expressionist; famous for painting subjects upside down to challenge perception.
  • Works display raw emotion, bold brushwork, and provocative imagery, influenced by German Expressionism, Mannerism, and outsider art.
  • Recurring themes include destruction, fragmentation, and post-war trauma.

Anselm Kiefer

Anselm Kiefer, Germany’s Spiritual Heroes 1973
  • Leading German Neo-Expressionist; paintings and installations deal with history, memory, and war, incorporating materials like lead, straw, and ash.
  • Evokes the complexity of German identity, using layered symbolism from myth, literature, religion, and recent history.
  • Highly textured surfaces suggest decay, trauma, and regeneration.

Jörg Immendorff

Jörg Immendorff, Café Deutschland 1978
  • Politically engaged painter and sculptor; works address division and reunification in Germany.
  • Famous for “Café Deutschland” series—allegorical, theatrical compositions about Cold War tension.
  • Art combines lively brushwork, satire, and commitment to social critique.

A.R. Penck

A.R. Penck, Standart 1970s
  • Known for works with stick figures, signs, and symbols resembling cave art or pictograms.
  • Visual language tackled communication, cultural division, censorship, and Cold War reality.
  • Raw, bold, instantly recognisable style made his work a symbol of postwar expressionism.

Markus Lüpertz

Markus Lüpertz, Helm Dithyrambisch 1970
  • Created semi-abstract paintings with bold forms and mythological themes drawn from classic literature.
  • Works reflect the ambiguity and complexity of cultural identity.
  • Rich, striking colour and energetic brushwork distinguish his style.

Julian Schnabel

Julian Schnabel, The Sea 1981
  • American leader of Neo-Expressionism; known for massive “plate paintings” (broken dishes on canvas) and flamboyant gesture.
  • Blend of historic reference, personal narrative, and dramatic materials.
  • His cross-disciplinary approach includes painting, film, and design.

Christopher Le Brun

Christopher Le Brun, Song of the Siren 1983
  • British painter, printmaker, sculptor known for lyrical abstraction and texture.
  • Works blend myth, landscape, music, and blur lines between figuration and abstraction.
  • Has played a key role as both artist and educator in contemporary British painting.

Paula Rego

Paula Rego, The Maids 1987
  • Portuguese-British artist celebrated for narrative paintings and pastels, tackling gender, power, and social injustice.
  • Draws on folk tales, literature, and personal stories; figures show psychological depth and agency.
  • Known for advocating for women’s rights through her art’s symbolism and storytelling.

Philip Guston

Philip Guston, Head and Bottle 1975
  • American painter who transitioned from Abstract Expressionism to a cartoon-like, figurative style.
  • Later work features recurring symbols—hoods, shoes, clocks—linked to political and personal themes.
  • His bold embrace of narrative and symbolism opened doors for Neo-Expressionist painting.

Frank Auerbach

Frank Auerbach, Head of Julia 1978
  • British artist noted for thick paint layers and repeated sittings for portraits and city scenes.
  • Paintings convey intense physicality, emotional presence, and deep engagement with subject.
  • Major figure in postwar British art, focused on materiality and process.

David Hockney

David Hockney, A Bigger Splash 1967
  • One of Britain’s leading artists, known for vivid paintings, drawings, and iPad works exploring perception and identity.
  • Innovative with colour, perspective, and technology; iconic for swimming pool imagery and digital art.
  • Spans Pop, modern, and contemporary styles; continues to influence international art.

Leon Kossoff

Leon Kossoff, Head of Philip 1967
  • School of London painter known for heavily textured, expressive portraits and cityscapes.
  • Often revisited the same subjects and places over time, deepening connection.
  • Focused on the act of painting and everyday emotional resonance.

Jean-Michel Basquiat

Jean-Michel Basquiat, Untitled 1981
  • Rose from New York’s graffiti scene to global fame; blended text, street art, and intense imagery.
  • Explored themes of race, identity, and power using symbols and references to African-American culture.
  • His raw energy, originality, and social commentary define 1980s art.

Eric Fischl

Eric Fischl, Self Portrait 1977
  • American painter of psychologically charged figurative scenes.
  • Depicts suburban life, intimacy, social tension, and vulnerability in a realist-expressive style.
  • Work noted for narrative complexity and dark exploration of contemporary society.

David Salle

David Salle, The Musician 1983
  • Known for layered paintings: fragments of figures, text, and images from diverse #sources and styles.
  • Creates ambiguity and complex visual narratives, reflecting postmodern pluralism.
  • Challenges traditional meaning, linear storytelling, and artistic identity.

Marlene Dumas

Marlene Dumas, The Teacher (Sub a) 1987
  • South African-Dutch artist famous for emotionally powerful, figurative paintings.
  • Based on found images, her art tackles identity, race, sexuality, and human experience.
  • Loose, gestural brushwork enhances psychological impact and social commentary.

Three Short Points for Students

  • New Expressionist and New Image artists revived strong colour, thick brushwork, and mixed techniques to express emotion and narrative.
  • Recognisable imagery and figures return, but are often distorted or cartoon-like to tell stories or comment on society.
  • The movement rebelled against rules, broke boundaries between media and styles, and made art that was both personal and engaged with the wider world.

Glossary for Students

  • Gestural: Art made using visible, expressive movements of the hand or brush.
  • Figurative: Art that shows real objects or people (not just shapes or patterns).
  • Mixed media: Using more than one material or technique in one work of art.
  • Distortion: Deliberately changing the normal appearance of an object, often to show feeling or make a point.
