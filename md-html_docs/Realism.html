Realism (c. 1848–1890)

Realism was an art movement devoted to showing life honestly and directly, without glossing over its difficulties or exaggerating its beauty. Realist artists focused on ordinary people and daily life, painting the world just as they saw it.

Key Themes & Ideas

Truthful Depiction of Everyday Life

  • Realist artists wanted to show the world as it truly was, without idealising or exaggerating their subjects.
  • They painted scenes of real people—workers, labourers, and daily life, including challenging or uncomfortable topics.

Focus on Contemporary, Working-Class Subjects

  • Rather than focusing on myths, heroes, or the past, they chose modern, everyday topics straight from life.
  • They highlighted the struggles and work of the poor and working class, making “ordinary” people the stars of serious art.

Attention to Detail & Direct Observation

  • Artists used close observation and careful technique, creating paintings that looked almost photographic in their detail and natural lighting.
  • Realist works avoided dramatic gestures—everything feels honest, grounded, and true to life.

Contextual Information

Main Locations

  • France (especially Paris)
  • Russia
  • Other parts of Europe

Previous Art Movements

  • Romanticism (emotional and dramatic—Realism was a reaction against it)
  • Neoclassicism (classical subjects and ideal beauty)

Culture & Politics

  • The 1848 Revolutions and rise of democracy
  • The Industrial Revolution: rapid urban change, social challenges, new class divisions
  • Increasing concern for social reform and the daily lives of ordinary people

Writers & Composers

Writers

  • Honoré de Balzac
  • Gustave Flaubert
  • Émile Zola (all focused on real life and society)

Composers

  • Modest Mussorgsky
  • Giuseppe Verdi (sometimes reflected Realist themes in opera)

Inventions & Technology

  • Photography (inspired artists to capture real scenes with accuracy)
  • Printing and journalism (spread Realist ideas widely)
  • New building materials: iron and steel

Typical Fashions

  • Men: Frock coats, waistcoats, cravats, top hats; later, sack suits
  • Women: Full skirts with crinolines or bustles, fitted bodices, bonnets, and shawls; fashion signalled class

Artists & Period

  • Period: 1848–1890 (core years)

Gustave Courbet

Gustave Courbet, A Burial at Ornans 1849–1850
  • Leader of the Realist movement in France, known for bold, honest depictions of working-class life.
  • Painted large canvases of ordinary people, elevating them to the scale of history paintings.
  • Used thick, tactile paint and the palette knife to give his works texture and reality.
  • Active politically—his radical approach inspired Impressionism and modern art.

Jean-François Millet

Jean-François Millet, The Gleaners 1857
  • One of the founders of the Barbizon School, painted rural peasants and farm workers with empathy and respect.
  • Works like The Gleaners show the dignity of manual labour and the realities of rural life.
  • His sense of monumentality and feeling influenced Realists and modern social painters.

Honoré Daumier

Honoré Daumier, The Third-Class Carriage c. 1862–1864
  • French painter, printmaker, and caricaturist famous for satirical prints and paintings critiquing politics and society.
  • Scenes from urban life blend pointed social commentary with lively brushwork.
  • His work is important for its mix of realism and social critique.

Jean-Baptiste-Camille Corot

Jean-Baptiste-Camille Corot, Ville-d'Avray 1867
  • French landscape painter who inspired both Realism and early Impressionism.
  • Blended careful observation with a poetic, atmospheric feeling.
  • Influenced later artists with his loose brushwork and focus on capturing light and mood outdoors (plein air).

Jules Bastien-Lepage

Jules Bastien-Lepage, Haymaking 1877
  • French painter known for sensitive, naturalistic depictions of peasants and country life.
  • Used direct observation and detail to show the reality of rural existence.
  • Famous works include Haymaking and Joan of Arc.

Vasily Perov

Vasily Perov, The Drowned Woman 1867
  • Russian painter, a founder of the Peredvizhniki (Wanderers), focused on everyday scenes and social issues.
  • Paintings often show the struggles and hardships of Russian life, like The Drowned Woman and Troika.
  • Helped define Russian social realism through close observation and empathy.

Vladimir Makovsky

Vladimir Makovsky, The Bank Crash 1881
  • Prominent Russian Realist, depicted the lives, struggles, and everyday scenes of ordinary people.
  • Works show narrative detail and psychological insight.
  • Addressed contemporary social issues and helped develop Russian social realism.

Ilya Repin

Ilya Repin, Barge Haulers on the Volga 1870–1873
  • Russia’s most celebrated realist—famous for powerful portraits, historical scenes, and observations of Russian society.
  • Barge Haulers on the Volga is a dramatic, honest look at labour and hardship.
  • Combined striking composition with deep psychological insight, influencing many later artists.

Three Short Points for Students

  • Realists painted real people and everyday life, even its harsh or challenging parts.
  • They focused on the struggle and dignity of working people, making ordinary life important in art.
  • Careful observation and natural detail made their pictures feel lifelike—rejecting fantasy or idealisation.

Glossary for Students

  • Idealise: To make something appear perfect or better than it is.
  • Romanticise: To make something more emotional or dramatic than reality.
  • Observation: Careful, direct looking at real life for accurate detail.
  • Working class: People who do manual or physical jobs, often with less wealth or status.
