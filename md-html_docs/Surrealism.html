Surrealism

Key Themes, Ideas, and Artistic Aspects

Exploration of the Unconscious and Dreams

  • Surrealism aimed to reveal the hidden world of the unconscious mind—dreams, desires, and fears invisible in waking life.
  • Techniques like automatism (drawing or writing without conscious control) let the imagination create spontaneous, dreamlike images.
  • This resulted in art that is mysterious, surprising, and sometimes unsettling.

Unexpected Juxtapositions and Symbolism

  • Surrealist artists merged unrelated objects or ideas in bizarre and surprising ways, defying logic and ordinary reality.
  • These unexpected combinations make viewers question what they see, often using symbols to explore themes like desire, fear, identity, and the irrational.

Rebellion Against Rationalism and Tradition

  • Surrealism protested against the rational thinking and traditions that many believed had led to the horrors of World War I.
  • The movement celebrated imagination, chance, and the irrational, striving to unite dream and reality into a new “surreality.”

Contextual Information

Main Locations

  • Paris was the main centre; Surrealism spread across Europe and the USA.

Relevant Previous Art Movements

  • Dada: Anti-art, rejected logic and embraced chance
  • Symbolism: Focused on dreams and emotions
  • Cubism and Expressionism: Provided new techniques and ideas

Relevant Culture and Politics

  • Developed between the World Wars—a time of instability and social change.
  • Many Surrealists were left-wing, inspired by Marxism and political revolution.
  • They opposed the rationalism blamed for conflict and destruction.

Contemporary Inventions and Technology

  • Photography and film—fast-growing and influential for Surrealist imagery and ideas
  • New printing and publishing technologies spread Surrealist ideas
  • Psychoanalysis (Freud’s theories) was a major influence

Typical Fashions of the Day

  • Men: Suits, narrow ties, fedoras or bowler hats, slicked-back hair
  • Women: Flapper dresses (1920s); later, more elegant tailored styles, bobbed or carefully waved hair

Period

  • 1920–1940 (core years of the movement and its greatest influence)

Key Artists

André Breton (poet and theorist)

  • Principal founder and theorist; wrote the Surrealist Manifesto (1924).
  • Champion of automatism, dreams, and the unconscious as creative sources.
  • United and shaped the movement through leadership and writing.

Max Ernst

Max Ernst, The Elephant Celebes 1921
  • German pioneer of both Dada and Surrealism, known for inventive collage and techniques like frottage and grattage.
  • Created dreamlike images filled with surprise, juxtaposition, and the irrational.
  • Ernst’s experiments influenced the course of modern art.

Salvador Dalí

Salvador Dalí, The Persistence of Memory 1931
  • Spanish Surrealist, famous for technical skill, imagination, and bizarre, dreamlike images—melting clocks, distorted landscapes, and more.
  • Worked in painting, sculpture, film, and literature, making Surrealism world-famous.
  • Dalí’s personality and showmanship made him an icon of modern art.

Luis Buñuel (film director)

  • Spanish filmmaker, worked closely with Dalí on films like Un Chien Andalou.
  • Known for shocking, dreamlike sequences and critiques of society and religion.
  • Helped shape avant-garde and mainstream cinema worldwide.

Man Ray

Man Ray, Le Violon d'Ingres 1924
  • American artist and photographer known for experimental work—especially rayographs (photograms without a camera).
  • Active in Dada and Surrealism, produced iconic works in multiple media.
  • His techniques and playful style helped define avant-garde art.

Hans (Jean) Arp

Hans (Jean) Arp, Sculpture c. 1930
  • Founder of Dada and Surrealism, created abstract, organic forms in sculpture and collage.
  • Embraced chance and spontaneity, reflecting growth and transformation in nature.
  • His biomorphic shapes bridged abstract and Surrealist fantasy.

André Masson

  • French painter/illustrator, known for automatic drawings exploring the unconscious mind.
  • Used unusual materials—sand, glue—for chance effects.
  • His energetic lines and themes explored mythology, violence, and eroticism.

Joan Miró

Joan Miró, Harlequin’s Carnival 1924–1925
  • Catalan painter, known for playful, abstract works with biomorphic shapes and vivid colours.
  • Inspired by dreams, childhood, and the subconscious.
  • Created a uniquely imaginative visual language.

Yves Tanguy

Yves Tanguy, Indefinite Divisibility 1942
  • French painter, known for meticulously painted, surreal landscapes filled with mysterious, organic shapes.
  • Work evokes dreamlike strangeness, often inspired by automatic drawing.
  • Leader of Surrealist painting’s mysterious side.

Giorgio de Chirico

Giorgio de Chirico, The Enigma of an Autumn Afternoon 1910
  • Italian artist whose metaphysical paintings anticipated Surrealism, using eerie cityscapes and enigmatic statues.
  • Explored mystery, the uncanny, and nostalgia with classical imagery and deep shadows.
  • Inspired Surrealists such as Dalí and Magritte.

Méret Oppenheim

Méret Oppenheim, Object (Le Déjeuner en fourrure) 1936
  • Swiss artist best known for “Object”—a fur-covered teacup, iconic Surrealist sculpture.
  • Work explores transformation, eroticism, and the uncanny using everyday objects.
  • Her feminist and inventive approach influenced later generations.

René Magritte

René Magritte, The Treachery of Images 1929
  • Belgian painter famous for witty, thought-provoking images that challenge reality and language.
  • Placed ordinary objects in unusual contexts and questioned what is real or represented.
  • Influenced contemporary art’s interest in illusion and perception.

Leonora Carrington

Leonora Carrington, The Temptation of Saint Anthony 1947
  • British-born painter and writer whose fantastical art draws on myth, magic, and personal symbolism.
  • Features hybrid creatures and mysterious narratives, especially in her Mexican years.
  • Central Surrealist in Mexico, blending literature and visual art.

Dorothea Tanning

Dorothea Tanning, Eine Kleine Nachtmusik 1943
  • American painter, sculptor, and writer, known for enigmatic, dreamlike art exploring subconscious themes.
  • Created surreal, often disturbing scenes in paintings and later explored more abstract, poetic work.
  • Also contributed poetry and prose to the movement.

Claude Cahun

Claude Cahun, Self-Portrait c. 1928
  • French artist, writer, and photographer focused on self-portraiture exploring identity, gender, and transformation.
  • Work was radical, anticipating conceptual art and performance.
  • Cahun is an LGBTQ+ icon for her avant-garde exploration of self and society.

Relevant Writers and Composers

Writers

  • André Breton (leader and theorist)
  • Louis Aragon
  • Paul Éluard

Composers

  • Erik Satie
  • Later, experimental composers who matched Surrealism’s mood

Three Short Points for Students

  • Surrealism explored dreams and the unconscious, using spontaneous techniques to create strange, dreamlike images.
  • It combined unrelated objects in surprising ways, offering new perspectives on reality and the mind.
  • The movement rebelled against tradition and rationality, aiming to unite imagination and real life into “surreality.”

Glossary for Students

  • Unconscious: The part of the mind that holds thoughts and feelings we are not aware of
  • Automatism: Making art without planning—letting the hand move freely to reveal hidden thoughts
  • Juxtaposition: Placing two things side by side, especially those that don’t normally go together
  • Surreality: A new reality that combines dreams and real life
  • Surrealism: A movement that changed art by opening up new ways to express the mysterious and hidden sides of human experience—shaping painting, literature, film, and culture worldwide.
