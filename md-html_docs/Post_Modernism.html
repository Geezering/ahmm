Postmodernism

Key Themes and Ideas

Mixing Styles, Irony, and Playfulness

  • Postmodern artists blend different styles, times, and cultural references with humour and irony.
  • They mix “high” art (painting, sculpture) and “low” culture (advertising, comics, TV), often using parody and pastiche to mimic or remix existing works.
  • This approach questions seriousness and originality, encouraging playfulness and self-awareness in art.

Questioning Truth, Meaning, and Originality

  • Postmodernism is sceptical about art revealing universal truths or stable meanings.
  • Artists often use appropriation (borrowing other images) and repetition to show that all art is, in some way, a remix of past #sources.
  • Meaning is seen as changeable and dependent on context and interpretation.

Complexity, Contradiction, and Consumerism

  • Postmodern art is often layered and contradictory, reflecting the complexities of modern life.
  • Artists explore identity, gender, mass media, politics, and consumer culture, frequently using new technologies: video, installation, and performance.
  • Style, surface, and spectacle are often emphasised, with artists embracing popular and consumer imagery.

Context and Influences

Main Locations

  • New York
  • London
  • Paris
  • Other major Western cities
  • Japan and South East Asia

Relevant Previous Movements

  • Modernism (including Abstract Expressionism, Minimalism, Conceptual Art, Pop Art)
  • Dada, Fluxus, Situationist International (direct influences)

Culture and Politics

  • Growth of mass media, globalisation, and consumer culture
  • Movements: feminism, civil rights, postcolonialism; questioning of tradition and authority
  • Disillusionment with “grand narratives” of progress and certainty, especially after the 1960s

Technology and Fashion

  • Rise of video, TV, early computers, digital graphics, and photocopying
  • New materials: plastics and mixed media in art-making
  • Men and Women: Bold colours, graphic prints, oversized silhouettes, mixing vintage and new styles
  • Street fashion was influenced by punk, New Wave, and hip-hop, while designer labels and style statements grew in importance

Key Writers, Philosophers, and Composers

Writers, Philosophers, Theorists

  • Jean-François Lyotard
  • Jacques Derrida
  • Roland Barthes
  • Cultural critics: Frederic Jameson, Richard Rorty
  • Note: Jean Baudrillard is often linked to Postmodernism but actually critiqued it from a Marxist perspective

Composers

  • John Cage
  • Laurie Anderson
  • Brian Eno (noted for genre-mixing and use of new technology)

Period

  • 1968–1990 (core years for Postmodernism in art and design)

Key Artists and Groups

Fluxus

Fluxus Artists, Fluxus Festival 1962
  • International, interdisciplinary network founded by George Maciunas, active mainly in the 1960s and 1970s.
  • Rejected boundaries between disciplines; promoted performance, chance, humour, and DIY aesthetics.
  • Emphasised process, anti-commercialism, and audience participation—shaping the course of performance and conceptual art.
  • Key figures: Joseph Beuys, Nam June Paik, Yoko Ono, Wolf Vostell.

Situationist International

Situationist International, May Events, Paris 1968
  • Radical collective combining art, intellectual theory, and activism, active 1957–1972.
  • Campaigned against consumer society and the spectacle of media, aiming to disrupt everyday routines and challenge capitalist norms.
  • Influenced urbanism, political activism (notably May 1968), and the rise of conceptual and relational art.

Nam June Paik

Nam June Paik, Magnet TV 1965
  • Korean-American pioneer of video art and media installations; close to Fluxus.
  • Used TV sets, video synthesisers, and interactive installations to explore technology, art, and pop culture.
  • Helped shape the field of media art; collaborated with musicians and performers.

Robert Venturi

Robert Venturi, Vanna Venturi House 1964
  • American architect/theorist; leader of postmodern architecture.
  • Promoted complexity, contradiction, and references to history and popular culture.
  • Writings, like "Complexity and Contradiction in Architecture," shaped late-20th-century visual culture.

Jeff Koons

Jeff Koons, Balloon Dog (Magenta) 1994–2000
  • American artist famed for large sculptures using advertising and kitsch imagery (e.g. balloon animals in stainless steel).
  • Works question taste, consumerism, and what counts as art.
  • Embraced spectacle, meticulous fabrication, and controversy—his art blurs boundaries between high and low culture.

Allan McCollum

Allan McCollum, Collection of Seventy-Five Eight-by-Ten-Inch Drawings 1991
  • Conceptual artist known for serial, mass-produced objects and installations exploring uniqueness, value, and meaning.
  • Projects like “Surrogate Paintings” and “Perfect Vehicles” examine individual/collective identity through repetition and variation.
  • Art challenges conventions of art display and questions the viewer’s role.

Robert Gober

Robert Gober, Untitled Leg 1989–90
  • American sculptor/installation artist, handcrafts objects (sinks, legs, doors) mixing everyday with the surreal.
  • His art explores memory, sexuality, trauma, and vulnerability.
  • Gober’s works invite viewers to reflect on identity and the politics of space.

Peter Halley

Peter Halley, Untitled 1998
  • Painter/writer linked to Neo-Geo; known for geometric abstractions (cells, conduits, grids).
  • Uses Day-Glo colours and industrial materials to critique social systems and urban life.
  • Theoretical writing addresses postmodernism and digital culture.

Haim Steinbach

Haim Steinbach, supreme 2013
  • Displays ordinary consumer objects on custom shelves to question display, value, and meaning.
  • Raises questions about consumption, desire, and identity through everyday things.
  • Associated with appropriation and institutional critique.

Frank Stella

Frank Stella, Harran II 1967
  • Pioneering Minimalist and post-painterly abstractionist; early works featured geometric shapes, shaped canvases, and flatness.
  • Later made dynamic reliefs and sculptures focused on materiality and formal innovation.
  • Has bridged movements from modern to postmodern through continual experimentation.

Robert Longo

Robert Longo, Men in the Cities 1981
  • Best known for large, hyperrealistic charcoal drawings (often people in dramatic poses).
  • Explores power, violence, mass media, and the pressures of contemporary society.
  • Works across drawing, sculpture, photography, and film.

David Salle

David Salle, The Musician 1983
  • Neo-Expressionist and postmodern painter famous for layered compositions of disparate images.
  • Juxtaposes historical and contemporary references, reflecting ambiguity and pluralism in art.
  • Challenged traditional notions of narrative and meaning in painting.

Sherrie Levine

Sherrie Levine, After Walker Evans: 2 1981
  • Appropriates and re-photographs iconic works by male artists to question authorship, originality, and gender bias in art.
  • Active in debates about appropriation and cultural value.
  • Key figure for postmodern critique and feminist strategies.

Three Short Points for Students

  • Postmodern artists mix styles and cultural references—often using irony and humour to question what art is and who it's for.
  • They challenge originality and truth, often copying or remixing earlier art to show meaning is never fixed.
  • This movement celebrates contradiction, complexity, and consumer culture, using new technologies and exciting spectacle.

Glossary for Students

  • Irony: Using words or images to mean the opposite of what is stated, often for humour or criticism.
  • Pastiche: An artwork made by combining elements from different sources or styles.
  • Appropriation: Using imagery, themes, or ideas from other sources or creators.
  • Spectacle: Work that is visually impressive and attention-grabbing, often more about image than deeper meaning.
  • Consumer culture: A society focused on shopping, brands, and images.
