De Stijl: Key Facts and Context

Main Locations

  • The Netherlands, especially Leiden, Laren, and Utrecht

Relevant Previous Art Movements

  • Cubism: Breaking down objects into geometric forms
  • Futurism: Focus on modernity and abstraction
  • Art Nouveau: Stylised, decorative art (which De Stijl reacted against)

Culture and Politics

  • Developed after World War I as a reaction to chaos and destruction
  • Artists wanted to build a new, harmonious society through art, design, and architecture
  • Utopian ideals: Believed art could help create a better and more ordered world

Relevant Writers and Composers

  • Theo van Doesburg: Artist, poet, critic, and editor of the De Stijl journal, wrote manifestos and articles
  • M.H.J. Schoenmaekers: Philosopher whose ideas about mathematics and spirituality influenced De Stijl artists
  • Composer: No official De Stijl composer, but Piet Mondrian was inspired by jazz and classical music

Contemporary Inventions and Technology

  • New materials in architecture and furniture (steel, glass, plywood)
  • Advances in printing and publishing (spread De Stijl ideas through magazines and manifestos)
  • Modernist furniture and architecture (e.g., Rietveld’s Red and Blue Chair)

Typical Fashions of the Day

  • Men: Suits, waistcoats, hats, short hair; some artists preferred practical clothing in the studio
  • Women: Long skirts and blouses in the 1910s, moving to shorter, simpler, and more functional styles in the 1920s

Artists and Period

  • Period: 1917–1931
  • Key artists: Theo van Doesburg, Piet Mondrian, Vilmos Huszár, Bart van der Leck, Gerrit Rietveld, Robert van ’t Hoff, J.J.P. Oud

Key Themes, Ideas, and Artistic Aspects

Theo van Doesburg

Theo van Doesburg, Composition IX 1917
  • Believed in a universal language of straight lines, rectangles, and primary colours (red, blue, yellow), plus black, white, and grey
  • Promoted harmony in society through unified principles in art, architecture, and design
  • Founded and edited the De Stijl journal, encouraging collaboration between artists and architects

Piet Mondrian

Piet Mondrian, Tableau I 1921
  • Created artworks using only black vertical and horizontal lines and blocks of primary colours
  • Called his style “Neo-Plasticism”, avoiding any reference to the real world
  • Believed art could express universal truths and bring spiritual harmony

Gerrit Rietveld

Gerrit Rietveld, Red and Blue Chair 1917
  • Designed furniture and buildings using De Stijl principles, including the iconic Red and Blue Chair and the Rietveld Schröder House
  • Used simple geometric forms and primary colours to make functional objects into works of art
  • Demonstrated how De Stijl ideas could be applied to all areas of life, not just painting

In Simple Terms for Students

  • De Stijl artists aimed for a completely new visual style for a modern world after World War I
  • They used only straight lines, rectangles, and the three primary colours, along with black, white, and grey
  • The goal was to create harmony, order, and clarity in art, design, and architecture, believing this could help improve society

Three Short Points for Each Artist

Theo van Doesburg

  1. Used strict geometry and primary colours for a universal style
  2. Connected art, design, and architecture
  3. Spread De Stijl ideas through writing and collaboration

Piet Mondrian

  1. Painted using only straight lines and primary colours
  2. Sought perfect balance and harmony
  3. Believed art could express universal, spiritual truths

Gerrit Rietveld

  1. Designed furniture and buildings using De Stijl rules
  2. Used simple shapes and colours for practical beauty
  3. Made art and design part of everyday life

De Stijl Was a Movement

  • Aimed to make art and design simple, clear, and universal—hoping to improve society by creating harmony and order from painting to architecture
