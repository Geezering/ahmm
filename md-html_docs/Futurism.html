Futurism (1909–1918): Key Themes and Artistic Aspects

Core Ideas

Celebration of Speed, Technology, and Modern Life

  • Futurist artists like Filippo Tommaso Marinetti, Umberto Boccioni, Carlo Carrà, Gino Severini, and Giacomo Balla focused on the energy, movement, and dynamism of the modern world.
  • They celebrated machines, cars, trains, aeroplanes, and fast-paced city life, aiming to capture the excitement and power of new technologies in their art.

Rejection of the Past and Emphasis on Change

  • The Futurists wanted a complete break from tradition—calling museums "cemeteries" and demanding the destruction of old art to make way for the new.
  • Their manifestos glorified originality, violence, and revolution, seeing war as a way to sweep away outdated ideas and celebrate the future.

Innovative Artistic Techniques for Movement

  • To show speed and motion, Futurists used blurring, repetition, and “lines of force”—strong visual lines showing movement, borrowed from Cubism.
  • Their art often looks fragmented and vibrant, with overlapping forms and bright colours that suggest energy in action.
  • They experimented across painting, sculpture, literature, music, and even architecture to share their vision of a dynamic world.

Context

Main Locations

  • Italy—especially Milan, Turin, and Naples

Relevant Previous Movements

  • Neo-Impressionism and Cubism – Inspired their techniques, use of colour, and fragmented forms.
  • Symbolism and Realism – Futurists reacted against their nostalgia and sentimentality.

Culture and Politics

  • Early 20th-century Italy: rapid industrialisation, political unrest, and a drive for national renewal.
  • Many Futurists supported radical politics and Italian nationalism; some later aligned with Fascism.
  • The First World War both inspired and later disillusioned many Futurist artists.

Inventions and Technology

  • Automobiles (cars), trains, aeroplanes
  • New printing and mass media technologies
  • Electric lighting and industrial machinery

Fashion

  • Men: Tailored suits, hats, ties (reflecting an urban, modern look)
  • Women: Long dresses and elaborate hats, eventually shifting toward simpler, more practical styles matching the new, energetic age

Period and Key Artists

  • Period: 1909–1918

Filippo Tommaso Marinetti

Filippo Tommaso Marinetti, Founder of Futurism c. 1910
  • Founder and chief theorist of Futurism, author of the “Futurist Manifesto” (1909).
  • Promoted art that glorified violence, dynamism, and the machine age, even seeing war as a force for renewal.
  • His radical ideas and manifestos inspired and united Futurist artists, setting the tone for the movement’s avant-garde energy.

Umberto Boccioni

Umberto Boccioni, Unique Forms of Continuity in Space 1913
  • Leading Futurist painter and sculptor, capturing movement and dynamism of modern life in both two and three dimensions.
  • His work (e.g. Unique Forms of Continuity in Space) broke down solid forms to show speed and fluidity, using Cubist ideas but focusing on movement.
  • Boccioni’s theory and innovative art helped define Futurism’s visual style.

Carlo Carrà

Carlo Carrà, The Funeral of the Anarchist Galli 1911
  • Key Futurist known for bold colour, fragmented forms, and dynamic compositions representing urban chaos and energy.
  • Sought to convey "dynamic simultaneity," showing several states of mind or motion at once.
  • Contributed to Futurism’s focus on modernity and revolution, and later helped develop Metaphysical painting.

Gino Severini

Gino Severini, Dynamic Hierarchy of Automobile 1915
  • Painted scenes of dance, nightlife, and city excitement, using rhythmic lines and bright colours to show movement.
  • Brought Futurism to Paris, mixing in Cubist and Divisionist techniques.
  • Explored links between art, music, and the rhythms of modern machinery, spreading Futurism internationally.

Giacomo Balla

Giacomo Balla, Dynamism of a Dog on a Leash 1912
  • Pioneer in expressing speed, light, and motion through abstract, repeated lines and fragmented shapes.
  • Works such as Dynamism of a Dog on a Leash show Futurist love for capturing fleeting moments.
  • Experimented with colour, kinetic form, and was central to both the theory and practice of Futurism.

Related Writers and Composers

Writers

  • Filippo Tommaso Marinetti – Poet, founder of Futurism, and writer of the Futurist Manifesto.

Composers

  • Luigi Russolo – Created experimental “noise music” reflecting Futurist ideas.
  • Francesco Balilla Pratella – Composer and musical Futurist.

Glossary for Students

  • Dynamism: Being full of energy and constant movement.
  • Manifesto: A public declaration of beliefs or aims.
  • Lines of force: Strong artistic lines showing direction, energy, or movement.
  • Fragmented: Broken into parts or pieces, often to suggest movement or chaos.
