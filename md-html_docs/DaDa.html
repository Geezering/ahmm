Dada: Key Facts and Context

Main Locations:

  • Zürich (Switzerland)
  • Berlin (Germany)
  • New York City (USA)
  • Paris (France)
  • with activity also in Cologne, Barcelona, and Hannover[2][3][7]

Relevant previous art movements:

  • Cubism (breaking down objects into geometric forms)
  • Futurism (focus on modernity and speed)
  • Expressionism (emotional, dramatic art)
  • Pre-war avant-garde and anti-art ideas[2][3]

Relevant culture and politics:

  • Dada emerged during and after World War I, as a protest against the violence, nationalism, and logic that artists believed had led to the war[2][3][4]
  • Artists rejected traditional values and the seriousness of art, using nonsense, absurdity, and humour to shock society and question authority
  • The movement was linked to radical left-wing politics and anti-bourgeois (anti-middle class) attitudes[2][3]

Relevant writers and Composers:

Writers:

  • Hugo Ball (poet and founder)
  • Tristan Tzara (poet and manifesto writer)
  • Richard Huelsenbeck
  • and others[2][3]

Composers:

  • Erik Satie
  • Igor Stravinsky
  • and Arnold Schoenberg
  • whose experimental music was sometimes performed at Dada events[3]

Contemporary Inventions and Technology:

  • Use of collage (cutting and sticking images and text)
  • Potomontage (combining photos)
  • Ready-mades (ordinary objects presented as art)[2][3]
  • Typewriters
  • Early photography
  • Printing presses helped artists create and share their work quickly and widely

Typical Fashions of the Day:

  • Men: Suits, waistcoats, bowler hats, and sometimes more eccentric or theatrical costumes for performances
  • Women: Long skirts, blouses, and hats, with some Dada women adopting androgynous or unconventional styles, especially in artistic circles

Key Themes, Ideas, and Artistic Aspects

Marcel Duchamp

  • Introduced the idea of the “readymade”-ordinary objects (like a urinal or bicycle wheel) displayed as art to question what art really is[1][2]
  • Used irony and humour to challenge traditional art values and the seriousness of museums and galleries
  • His work encouraged artists to focus on ideas and concepts, not just skill or beauty

Hannah Höch

  • Pioneered photomontage, cutting and rearranging photographs from magazines and newspapers to create new, often satirical images[2][3]
  • Critiqued gender roles and politics, especially the position of women in society, through her art
  • Used randomness and chance, showing that art could be playful, critical, and made from everyday materials

Tristan Tzara

  • Wrote important Dada manifestos, using nonsense and absurdity to attack logic and traditional thinking[2][3]
  • Organised performances and poetry readings that involved chaos, sound poetry (nonsense words and noises), and audience participation
  • Believed that art should provoke, confuse, and make people question everything, not just please the eye

In Simple Terms for Students

  • Dada artists:
  • Were rebels who wanted to shock society and show that art could be anything, even a joke or a random object
  • They used nonsense, humour, and everyday materials to protest against war and the rules of “serious” art
  • Their work was about breaking boundaries, making people think, and showing that art could be found anywhere

Three Short Points for Each Artist

Marcel Duchamp

  1. Pioneered the concept of the "readymade," turning ordinary objects like a urinal (Fountain, 1917) into art to challenge traditional definitions of art and the artist's role.[1][5][6]
  2. Created provocative works such as L.H.O.O.Q. (Mona Lisa with a mustache) that mocked revered masterpieces and questioned artistic value, originality, and authorship.[1][5]
  3. Was a central figure in Dada's anti-art movement, using irony, humor, and intellectual play to undermine artistic conventions and laying the groundwork for later movements like Surrealism and Conceptual Art.[2][3][4]

Francis Picabia

  1. Was a key link between Dada groups in Paris, Zurich, and New York, helping to internationalize the movement and spread its ideas.[2]
  2. Created mechanomorphic (machine-like) drawings and paintings that satirized modern technology and the mechanization of society.
  3. Embraced absurdity and constant stylistic change, using irony and parody to challenge artistic seriousness and expectations.

Hugo Ball

  1. Co-founded the Cabaret Voltaire in Zurich in 1916, which became the birthplace and central hub of Dada activities.[2]
  2. Invented "sound poetry"—abstract vocal performances using invented words and nonsensical syllables to break free from logical language.
  3. Wrote the Dada Manifesto, articulating the movement's anti-war, anti-bourgeois, and anti-art stance.

Emmy Hennings

  1. Co-founded the Cabaret Voltaire with Hugo Ball and was a driving force in early Zurich Dada.[2]
  2. Performed poetry, songs, and cabaret acts that embodied Dada's spirit of rebellion and experimentation.
  3. Her writings and performances often addressed themes of marginalization, social critique, and personal struggle.

Hans Arp

  1. Developed abstract collage and relief works using chance operations, emphasizing randomness and spontaneity.[2]
  2. Created biomorphic (organic, natural) forms that blurred boundaries between painting and sculpture.
  3. Was a founding member of both Zurich and later Cologne Dada, contributing poetry and visual art.

Raoul Hausmann

  1. Pioneered the photomontage technique, combining photographs and text to create satirical and politically charged images.
  2. Was a leading figure in Berlin Dada, using art to critique German society and militarism after World War I.
  3. Explored sound poetry and invented the optophone, a device for converting images to sounds.

Hannah Höch

  1. Was the only prominent woman in the Berlin Dada group and a master of photomontage, using cut-up images to critique gender roles and politics.
  2. Her work often addressed issues of women's emancipation and the absurdities of Weimar society.
  3. Blended humor and sharp social commentary in both her art and her writings.

Johannes Baader

  1. Known as the "Oberdada" (Super-Dada), he was a central organizer and provocateur in Berlin Dada.
  2. Created large-scale assemblages and performances that mocked authority and bureaucracy.
  3. Used manifestos and public demonstrations to promote Dada's anti-establishment ideals.

Tristan Tzara

  1. Was a founding poet and theorist of Dada, writing manifestos and organizing events in Zurich and Paris.[2]
  2. Promoted chance and nonsense in poetry, famously advocating cutting up newspapers to create poems.
  3. Helped spread Dada internationally and later contributed to Surrealism.

Richard Huelsenbeck

  1. Co-founded Dada in Zurich and played a leading role in establishing Berlin Dada after returning to Germany.[2]
  2. Wrote manifestos and edited Dada publications, promoting radical political and artistic ideas.
  3. His performances and writings often used rhythm, noise, and satire to attack bourgeois culture.

George Grosz

  1. Created biting, satirical drawings and paintings that exposed the corruption and hypocrisy of Weimar Germany.
  2. Was a leading member of Berlin Dada and later the New Objectivity movement.
  3. Used caricature and grotesque imagery to critique militarism, capitalism, and authoritarianism.

John Heartfield

  1. Pioneered political photomontage, using manipulated photographs to attack fascism and Nazi propaganda.
  2. Worked closely with Berlin Dada and later the Communist press.
  3. His art combined visual impact with sharp political messages, influencing later protest art.

Man Ray

  1. Was a key figure in both New York and Paris Dada, experimenting with photography, film, and object-making.[2]
  2. Invented the "rayograph" (cameraless photograph), creating surreal images by placing objects directly on photographic paper.
  3. Blended Dada’s absurdity with Surrealism’s dreamlike imagery in both his art and films.

Kurt Schwitters

  1. Created "Merz" assemblages from found objects, developing a unique personal style related to but distinct from mainstream Dada.
  2. Used collage, poetry, and performance to explore themes of chaos, reconstruction, and everyday life.
  3. His work bridged Dada, Constructivism, and early installation art.

Hans Richter

  1. Was a pioneer of abstract film, creating some of the first experimental Dada movies.
  2. Participated in Zurich Dada and collaborated with artists like Viking Eggeling.
  3. Explored the relationship between visual art, movement, and rhythm.

Max Ernst

  1. Was a founding member of Cologne Dada, creating collages and assemblages that combined absurdity with dreamlike imagery.
  2. Experimented with automatic techniques and later became a major figure in Surrealism.
  3. Used humor, fantasy, and subversion to challenge reality and authority in his art.
