Dada: Key Facts and Context

Main Locations

  • Zürich (Switzerland)
  • Berlin (Germany)
  • New York City (USA)
  • Paris (France)
  • Also active in Cologne, Barcelona, and Hannover

Relevant Previous Art Movements

  • Cubism – Breaking down objects into geometric forms
  • Futurism – Focus on modernity and speed
  • Expressionism – Emotional and dramatic art
  • Pre-war avant-garde and anti-art ideas

Culture and Politics

  • Emerged during and after World War I as a protest against violence, nationalism, and rationalism—factors artists believed caused the war
  • Artists rejected traditional art values, using nonsense, absurdity, and humour to shock society and question authority
  • Linked to radical left-wing and anti-bourgeois attitudes

Contemporary Inventions and Technology

  • Collage: Cutting and sticking images and text together
  • Photomontage: Combining photographs in new ways
  • Readymades: Ordinary objects presented as art
  • Typewriters, early photography, and printing presses enabled rapid, wide sharing of Dada works

Typical Fashions of the Day

  • Men: Suits, waistcoats, bowler hats; sometimes more theatrical or eccentric costumes in performances
  • Women: Long skirts, blouses, hats; some adopted androgynous or unconventional styles in Dada circles

Artists and Period

  • Main period: 1915–1925

Marcel Duchamp

Marcel Duchamp, Fountain 1917
  • Introduced the “readymade”—ordinary objects such as a urinal or bicycle wheel displayed as art to challenge what art is
  • Used irony and humour to question artistic values and the seriousness of museums
  • Encouraged artists to focus on ideas and concepts, not just visual skill or beauty

Hannah Höch

Hannah Höch, Cut with the Kitchen Knife 1919
  • Pioneered photomontage, reorganising magazine and newspaper photos to create new, often satirical images
  • Critiqued gender roles and politics, especially women’s place in society
  • Used chance and randomness: art could be playful, critical, and made from everyday materials

Tristan Tzara

Tristan Tzara, Portrait by Man Ray c. 1920
  • Wrote key Dada manifestos, attacking logic and tradition with nonsense and absurdity
  • Organised performances and sound poetry, involving chaos and audience participation
  • Believed art should provoke and confuse, not just please the eye

Francis Picabia

Francis Picabia, Here, This is Stieglitz Here 1915
  • Key link between Dada groups in Paris, Zurich, and New York
  • Created machine-inspired (“mechanomorphic”) art satirising modern technology
  • Embraced absurdity and constant stylistic change, challenging art seriousness with irony and parody

Hugo Ball

Hugo Ball, Cabaret Voltaire performance 1916
  • Co-founded Cabaret Voltaire in Zurich, the birthplace of Dada
  • Invented “sound poetry”—nonsense performances with invented words and syllables
  • Wrote the Dada Manifesto, expressing anti-war, anti-bourgeois, and anti-art beliefs

Key Themes, Ideas, and Artistic Aspects

  • Readymades: Everyday objects presented as art
  • Photomontage: Cut-and-paste images from media with social or political critique
  • Anti-art: Embraced nonsense, humour, and absurdity to rebel against established values
  • Chance and collage: Used randomness in creation to question meaning and skill in art
  • Performance and poetry: Live events, sound poetry, and collaboration were central

In Simple Terms for Students

  • Dada artists were rebels who wanted to shock, confuse, or amuse the world
  • They believed art could be anything, even a joke, a random object, or a wild performance
  • Dada protested against war, logic, and the rules of “serious” art
  • Their work aimed to break boundaries and make people think for themselves
