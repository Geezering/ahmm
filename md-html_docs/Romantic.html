Romanticism (c. 1770–1850)

Romanticism was a movement that placed strong emotion, the imagination, and the artist’s personal vision at the heart of art and culture. Romantic artists aimed to capture powerful feelings, wild nature, and the spirit of rebellion in times of great change.

Key Themes & Ideas

Emotion, Imagination & Individualism

  • Romantic artists made personal feelings and the imagination central to their work.
  • They believed in expressing their own emotions, not just following rules or imitating ancient art.
  • Their art is often original, dramatic, and deeply felt.

Nature & the Sublime

  • Nature became a powerful inspiration—beautiful, wild, and sometimes frightening.
  • Artists painted dramatic landscapes, storms, and ruins to evoke awe (the “sublime” means something so vast it almost overwhelms you).
  • Nature was seen as a source of truth and escape from a rapidly changing, industrial world.

Rebellion, Nationalism & the Past

  • Romantics reacted against Enlightenment reason and Industrial Revolution progress.
  • They celebrated national identity, folklore, and the heroism of history (especially the Middle Ages).
  • Themes of rebellion, freedom, and the struggles of ordinary people are common.

Contextual Information

Main Locations

  • Across Europe, especially France, Germany, Britain, and Spain.

Previous Movements

  • Neoclassicism (order, reason, classical subjects—Romanticism was a reaction against this).
  • Baroque (influenced drama and emotion).

Culture & Politics

  • The French Revolution and Napoleonic Wars
  • The rise of nationalism across Europe
  • The Industrial Revolution’s impact on people and nature
  • Renewed interest in folklore, medieval history, and the supernatural

Writers & Composers

Writers

  • Johann Wolfgang von Goethe
  • Lord Byron
  • William Wordsworth
  • Samuel Taylor Coleridge
  • Mary Shelley
  • Victor Hugo
  • The Brothers Grimm

Composers

  • Ludwig van Beethoven
  • Franz Schubert
  • Hector Berlioz
  • Felix Mendelssohn

Inventions & Technology

  • Steam engines and railways
  • Early factories and manufacturing
  • New printing and publishing methods
  • Portable paint tubes—these let artists paint outdoors (en plein air)

Fashion

  • Men: Tailcoats, waistcoats, high-collared shirts, breeches or trousers, and top hats
  • Women: High-waisted “empire” dresses (early 1800s), later fuller skirts, shawls, bonnets, and elaborate hairstyles

Key Artists of Romanticism

Théodore Géricault

Théodore Géricault, The Raft of the Medusa 1818–1819
  • French painter who helped define Romanticism through dramatic, emotionally charged work.
  • The Raft of the Medusa shows a real-life shipwreck and the struggle for survival, with unflinching realism and powerful emotion.
  • Challenged academic tradition by elevating modern, often disturbing subjects.

Caspar David Friedrich

Caspar David Friedrich, Wanderer above the Sea of Fog 1818
  • German painter known for poetic landscape scenes that evoke solitude and reflection.
  • Frequently uses lone figures in vast nature to symbolise the “sublime” and the search for meaning.
  • Famous for misty forests, ruins, and mysterious light.

Eugène Delacroix

Eugène Delacroix, Liberty Leading the People 1830
  • The leading Romantic painter in France, renowned for vibrant colour and dynamic style.
  • Created dramatic, emotional scenes including Liberty Leading the People (a symbol of revolution) and The Death of Sardanapalus.
  • Influenced the Impressionists with his expressive brushwork.

J.M.W. Turner

J.M.W. Turner, Rain, Steam, and Speed – The Great Western Railway 1844
  • Revolutionary British artist, famous for landscapes and seascapes painted with luminous colour and light.
  • Later works verge on abstraction, conveying immense power in nature and atmosphere.
  • Pushed the boundaries of painting and inspired Impressionism.

Francisco de Goya y Lucientes

Francisco de Goya, The Third of May 1808 1814
  • Spanish painter and printmaker whose career moved from pretty Rococo styles to disturbing, visionary works.
  • The Third of May 1808 and The Disasters of War explore violence, injustice, and psychological darkness.
  • Considered a forebear of both Romanticism and modernist art.

William Blake

William Blake, The Great Red Dragon and the Woman Clothed in Sun c. 1805–1810
  • English poet, painter, and printmaker known for his mystical, imaginative art and writing.
  • Combined poetry and image in “illuminated books” such as Songs of Innocence and of Experience.
  • Blake’s visionary, radical work influenced both literature and visual art.

Samuel Palmer

Samuel Palmer, The Magic Apple Tree 1830
  • British painter and printmaker, celebrated for mystical landscapes inspired by nature and the countryside around Shoreham.
  • Blended rich colour, pastoral tradition, and spiritual symbolism.
  • Part of the visionary circle around William Blake.

John Sell Cotman

John Sell Cotman, Greta Bridge, Yorkshire 1805
  • Key English watercolourist and etcher, leading figure in the Norwich School.
  • Known for subtle harmony of colour and clear, lyrical compositions of landscapes and buildings.
  • Influenced later developments in British landscape art.

Jean-Jacques Barre

Jean-Jacques Barre, Legion of Honour Medal 1852
  • French engraver and medallist, known as chief engraver at the Paris Mint.
  • Designed medals, coins, and stamps, shaping the visual identity of 19th-century France.
  • His precise, elegant designs remain important in the history of official art and currency.

John Constable

John Constable, The Hay Wain 1821
  • Pioneering English landscape painter, famous for naturalistic depictions of rural England, especially Suffolk.
  • Used fresh, lively brushstrokes to capture light, changing weather, and atmosphere.
  • Inspired later artists including the Barbizon School and Impressionists.

Arnold Böcklin

Arnold Böcklin, Isle of the Dead 1883
  • Swiss Symbolist painter known for mysterious, haunting scenes blending myth, allegory, and landscape.
  • Isle of the Dead became an icon of mystery and melancholy.
  • His work bridges Romantic fantasy and the beginnings of Surrealist art.

Three Short Points for Students

  • Romantic artists focused on intense emotion and imagination, breaking away from traditional rules.
  • They celebrated the wildness of nature, dramatic landscapes, and the “sublime” to inspire awe.
  • Romanticism explored themes of rebellion, national pride, folklore, and the mysteries of history.

Glossary for Students

  • The Sublime: Something so powerful or vast that it fills you with awe or even fear.
  • Subjectivity: The idea that everyone’s emotions and thoughts matter and are unique.
  • En plein air: Painting directly outdoors in nature.
  • Nationalism: Pride in, and support for, your country's identity and culture.
