Post-Impressionism

Core Ideas

Personal Expression

  • Artists focused on expressing feelings, memories, and ideas through their work—not just copying what they saw.
  • They used bold, sometimes unnatural colours and dramatic brushstrokes to show mood and emotion.

Experimentation with Form and Colour

  • Many experimented with shapes and new ways of arranging pictures, often using patterns or geometry.
  • Cézanne used structured brushwork and simplified forms; Seurat and Signac developed pointillism—painting with dots of pure colour.

Symbolism and Abstraction

  • Post-Impressionists introduced symbols and abstract elements—using colours, shapes, and composition to suggest deeper meanings or personal stories.
  • This paved the way for movements like Cubism and Expressionism where meaning became as important as realistic appearance.

Context

Main Locations

  • Paris (and much of France—Provence, Brittany)
  • Belgium, Norway, Holland
  • America, Canada, Australia, and elsewhere as ideas spread internationally

Previous Art Movements

  • Impressionism – on light, fleeting moments, and naturalism
  • Realism – everyday subjects painted with sincerity
  • Symbolism – for artists seeking deeper, hidden meanings

Cultural & Political Climate

  • Late 19th-century Europe: rapid industrial growth, urban expansion, and shifts in society
  • Interest in psychology, the unconscious, dreams, and personal experience was rising

Inventions and Technology

  • Portable paint tubes (made painting outdoors easier)
  • New pigments and colour theory
  • Advances in printing and photography

Fashion

  • Men: Three-piece suits, bowler hats, moustaches
  • Women: High-necked blouses, long skirts, corsets, elaborate hats

Main Post-Impressionist Artists

Paul Cézanne

Paul Cézanne, Mont Sainte-Victoire c. 1905
  • Known as the 'father of Post-Impressionism,' Cézanne aimed to bring structure and order to painting.
  • Broke objects into simple shapes (cylinders, spheres), giving paintings a solid, almost sculptural feel.
  • Used carefully planned colour and brushwork to create harmony.
  • Inspired the development of Cubism.

Vincent van Gogh

Vincent van Gogh, The Starry Night 1889
  • Famous for emotional, swirling brushwork and intense colours.
  • Used paint in thick layers and chose colours to express feelings, not just appearances.
  • His energetic style makes his paintings instantly recognisable.
  • Although not successful while alive, his art is now celebrated worldwide.

Paul Gauguin

Paul Gauguin, Where Do We Come From? What Are We? Where Are We Going? 1897
  • Used colour and shape to create a sense of mystery and symbolism.
  • Painted scenes from Tahiti with bold areas of flat colour and strong outlines.
  • Sought to express dreams, myths, and ideas beyond everyday life.
  • Influenced many artists who wanted to move beyond naturalism.

Georges Seurat

Georges Seurat, A Sunday Afternoon on the Island of La Grande Jatte 1884–1886
  • Invented pointillism: painting with dots of pure colour that blend when viewed from a distance.
  • Carefully planned colours and composition for harmony and order.
  • His most famous paintings are calm, organised, and highly structured.

Henri de Toulouse-Lautrec

Henri de Toulouse-Lautrec, At the Moulin Rouge 1892–1895
  • Famous for paintings and posters of Parisian nightlife, such as cabarets and dancers.
  • Used bold lines, flat areas of colour, and striking compositions.
  • Influenced by Japanese prints and modern posters.
  • His work gives a vivid picture of late 19th-century Paris.

Key Ideas: Perceptual and Conceptual Art

What Is Post-Impressionism?

  • Post-Impressionist artists all had separate approaches. There is no single style, but they shared the goal of moving beyond direct observation to express ideas and emotions.
  • Post-Impressionists are often described as 'conceptual artists'—interested in ideas and meaning, not just appearance.
  • Impressionism was 'perceptual': it aimed to capture what a scene looks like for a moment in time, like a photograph.
  • By contrast, conceptual artists used images as diagrams or maps—presenting thoughts and ideas that could not be seen from one viewpoint.

Perceptual vs Conceptual Art

  • Perceptual art: Captures how a place appears at one time and in one light (like a photo).
  • Conceptual art: Shows ideas, plans, or information—sometimes combining different viewpoints, or showing things that cannot be seen directly.
