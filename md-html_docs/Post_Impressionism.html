Post Impressionism

Key Themes, Ideas, and Artistic Aspects

Personal Expression and Emotional Impact

  • Post-Impressionist artists moved beyond simply capturing what they saw, focusing instead on expressing their own feelings, memories, and ideas through their art
  • They often used bold, unnatural colours and dramatic brushstrokes to show emotion or mood, rather than just copying real life

Experimentation with Form, Colour, and Structure

  • Many Post-Impressionists experimented with geometric shapes, patterns, and new ways of organising their paintings
  • Some, like Cézanne, used structured brushwork and simplified forms, while others, like Seurat and Signac, developed pointillism (painting with tiny dots of colour)

Symbolism and Abstraction

  • Post-Impressionists often included symbolic or abstract elements in their work, using colour, shape, and composition to suggest deeper meanings or personal stories
  • This set the stage for later movements like Cubism and Expressionism, as artists became more interested in what art could mean, not just what it could show

Contextual information

Main location(s):

  • Paris and other parts of France (especially Provence and Brittany)
  • Belgium
  • Norway
  • Holland
  • America, Canada, Australia and beyond as artists travelled and exchanged ideas

Relevant previous art movements:

  • Impressionism (focus on light and everyday scenes, but more naturalistic)
  • Realism (depicting ordinary life)
  • Symbolism (for artists interested in deeper meanings)

Relevant culture and politics:

  • Late 19th-century Europe: rapid industrialisation, urban growth, and social change
  • Growing interest in psychology, the unconscious, and personal experience

Relevant writers and Composers:

Writers:

  • Émile Zola
  • Stéphane Mallarmé
  • Paul Verlaine (explored emotion and symbolism)

Composers:

  • Claude Debussy
  • Erik Satie (experimented with mood and impression in music)

Contemporary inventions and technology:

  • Portable paint tubes (helped artists paint outdoors)
  • Colour theory and new pigments
  • Advances in printing and photography

Typical fashions of the day for men and women:

  • Women: High-necked blouses, long skirts, corsets, and elaborate hats

Main Post-Impressionist Artists

Paul Cézanne

  • Cézanne is often called the “father of Post-Impressionism.” He wanted to bring structure and order back into painting, which he felt Impressionism lacked.
  • Cézanne used careful brushstrokes and often broke objects down into simple shapes, like cylinders and spheres, giving his paintings a solid, almost sculptural feel. His use of colour was bold, but always carefully planned.
  • Cézanne’s approach influenced many later artists, especially those who developed Cubism.

Vincent van Gogh

  • Van Gogh is famous for his vibrant colours and swirling, energetic brushwork.
  • He used paint in thick layers and chose colours not just to show what things looked like, but to express his feelings and moods.
  • Van Gogh’s paintings often seem full of movement and emotion, making them instantly recognisable.
  • Although he struggled with mental health and was not successful in his lifetime, his work is now some of the most celebrated in art history.

Paul Gauguin

  • Gauguin broke away from painting what he saw in front of him and instead used colour and shape to create a sense of mystery and symbolism.
  • He often painted scenes from his travels, especially in Tahiti, using flat areas of bold colour and strong outlines.
  • Gauguin wanted his art to show dreams, myths, and ideas, not just everyday life.
  • His style influenced many artists who wanted to go beyond simply copying nature.

Georges Seurat

  • Seurat developed a technique called pointillism, where he painted with tiny dots of pure colour that blend together when seen from a distance.
  • He was very scientific in his approach, carefully planning how colours would interact to create light and shadow.
  • Seurat’s paintings are calm and organised, with a sense of order and harmony. His most famous work, “A Sunday Afternoon on the Island of La Grande Jatte,” shows this technique at its best.

Henri de Toulouse-Lautrec

  • Toulouse-Lautrec is known for his lively scenes of Parisian nightlife, especially in places like the Moulin Rouge.
  • He used bold lines and bright colours to capture the energy and excitement of cabarets, dancers, and performers.
  • His style was influenced by posters and Japanese prints, making his work look modern and striking.
  • Toulouse-Lautrec’s art gives us a glimpse into the vibrant world of late 19th-century Paris.

Post Impressionism was..

  • These artists all reacted against Impressionism in different ways. It is difficult, then, to say ‘what Post Impressionism was’ since all of these artists have separate ideas, programmes and methods.
  • We can say that they shared a desire to go beyond just painting what they saw, using colour, shape, and technique to express ideas, emotions, and personal visions.
  • We can also say that they were all conceptual artists and not perceptual artists.
  • Impressionism was a perceptual art form: it was painting that showed you what you see before you at a certain time and a particular place. Like a photograph does.
  • By contrast, conceptual art introduces the idea of painting and identifying ideas, structures, and concepts that are not visual. What this means is that the picture does not function as a record of how the subject is perceived at a particular and unique time and place, but instead, conceptual paintings serve as diagrams of ideas that people can share about things.

Perceptual and Conceptual art

  • Perceptual art: a photo of a location at a given time under given lighting conditions, weather, etc
  • Conceptual art: a diagram, a map or a plan that can present information from different view points or schematically show what can’t be seen from one viewpoint.
