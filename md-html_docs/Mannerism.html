Mannerism (1550–1600)

Mannerism followed the High Renaissance, known for its elegance, complexity, and emotional intensity. Artists deliberately moved away from naturalism to create sophisticated, sometimes strange images designed for educated audiences.

Three Key Points

Artificiality, Elegance & Distortion

  • Artists like Pontormo, Bronzino, and Parmigianino rejected the balance and harmony of the High Renaissance.
  • Their work is intentionally artificial, with elongated bodies, unusual poses, and sophisticated, almost mannered detail.
  • Mannerist art is often stylish and decorative—displaying skill rather than depicting real life.

Complex Composition & Unusual Space

  • Mannerist paintings have crowded or strangely empty spaces, and compositions can feel tense or unbalanced.
  • Backgrounds may have odd or impossible perspectives, creating a sense of instability.
  • Unusual colour combinations and dramatic lighting add to the intensity and theatrical effect.

Intellectual Sophistication & Symbolism

  • Mannerist artists filled their work with clever references to mythology, classical art, and philosophy.
  • Puzzle-like scenes invite viewers to look for hidden meanings and enjoy the mystery, rather than just following a simple story.
  • This complexity appealed to wealthy and educated patrons who valued intellectual challenge and elegance.

Contextual Information

Main Locations

  • Italy (especially Florence, Rome, Venice), later spreading across Europe (Spain and Elizabethan England)

Relevant Previous Art Movements

  • High Renaissance (Leonardo, Michelangelo, Raphael)

Culture & Politics

  • Uncertainty and upheaval after the High Renaissance, such as the Sack of Rome and the Protestant Reformation.
  • Art became a way for artists and patrons to display learning and sophistication in a changing world.

Writers & Composers

Writers

  • Torquato Tasso
  • Ludovico Ariosto (famous for poetry and complex epic tales)

Composers

  • Luca Marenzio
  • Carlo Gesualdo (noted for expressive, sometimes startling harmonies)

Inventions & Technology

  • Improvements in oil painting and pigments allowed for richer colours and finer details.
  • The printing press continued to spread artistic and literary ideas quickly across Europe.

Typical Fashions

  • Men: Ornate doublets, hose, ruffs (stiff collars), and decorated fabrics.
  • Women: High-waisted gowns, long sleeves, structured bodices, elaborate jewellery, and dramatic hats or headpieces.

Artists & Period: 1550–1600

Pontormo

Pontormo, Deposition from the Cross 1525–1528
  • Early Mannerist leader who broke from classical harmony towards expressive, personal style.
  • Works have vivid colour, elongated bodies, and swirling, dynamic poses.
  • Often created compressed, ambiguous spaces and a strong sense of emotional intensity.
  • Influenced Bronzino and anticipated later expressive art movements.

Bronzino

Bronzino, Portrait of Eleonora of Toledo with her Son 1545
  • Pupil of Pontormo; refined Mannerism into a cool, polished, and elegant style.
  • Known for sophisticated court portraits with clear detail, controlled emotion, and decorative beauty.
  • Set the standard for Florentine portraiture as court painter to Cosimo I de’ Medici.
  • Works often seem restrained, intellectual, and full of symbolic references.

Parmigianino

Parmigianino, Madonna with the Long Neck c. 1535–1540
  • Famed for graceful, elongated forms, especially in religious scenes.
  • Compositions are elegant, with striking distortions and poetic, dreamlike beauty.
  • Pushed Mannerist distortion and sophistication to new heights.
  • His innovations influenced many later artists in Italy and beyond.

Tintoretto

Tintoretto, The Last Supper 1592–1594
  • Brought intense drama and dynamism to Venetian painting.
  • His works feature complex, theatrical compositions and bold perspectives.
  • Famous for rapid brushwork and striking use of light and shadow.
  • His approach set the stage for the Baroque and inspired later Venetian artists.

Paolo Veronese

Paolo Veronese, The Wedding at Cana 1562–1563
  • Known for grand, colourful paintings with complex compositions set in magnificent architectural surroundings.
  • Used vibrant colour and pageantry to create dramatic biblical and allegorical scenes.
  • His lively, decorative style became a hallmark of the Venetian Renaissance.
  • Master of connecting theatre and painting through scale and splendour.

El Greco

El Greco, The Burial of the Count of Orgaz 1586–1588
  • Developed a highly original style, blending Venetian colour, Byzantine iconography, and Mannerist form.
  • Famous for elongated figures, spiritual expressiveness, and dramatic colour and light.
  • His work focused on religious visions and mystical experiences.
  • Anticipated later movements like Expressionism and inspired artists far beyond his time.

Moroni

Giovanni Battista Moroni, The Tailor c. 1570
  • Best known for naturalistic portraits full of psychological insight and individuality.
  • Style stood out for its clarity and honest representation, contrasting with ornate Mannerist ideals.
  • Helped build the tradition of Italian portraiture through directness and technical skill.
  • Paintings often reveal the social status and personality of his sitters.
