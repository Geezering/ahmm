Analytic Cubism: Key Facts and Context

Main Locations

  • Paris, France was the central hub for Analytic Cubism.

Artistic Origins

  • Post-Impressionism (especially Paul Cézanne’s use of faceted forms and multiple viewpoints)
  • African art (inspired new ways of representing figures and objects with simplified, stylised shapes)

Cultural & Political Context

  • Early 20th-century Paris was a centre for artistic innovation and cultural exchange.
  • This period, just before the First World War, saw rapid change, new discoveries, and a move away from old traditions.

Contemporary Inventions and Technology

  • X-rays: Sparked the idea of seeing through objects and viewing from several angles at once.
  • Photography and early cinema: Challenged traditional ways of seeing and representing reality.

Typical Fashions of the Day

  • Men: Three-piece suits, bowler hats, and moustaches.
  • Women: Long skirts, blouses, wide-brimmed hats, and the beginnings of looser, more practical clothing.

Key Artists and Period

  • Period: Analytic Cubism was most active from approximately 1908 to 1912.

Pablo Picasso

Pablo Picasso, Girl with a Mandolin (Fanny Tellier) 1910
  • Co-founder of Cubism, alongside Georges Braque.
  • Broke down objects and people into geometric shapes and overlapping planes to show several viewpoints at once.
  • Used limited colours (browns, greys, ochres) to focus on form and structure, not bright palettes.
  • Challenged viewers to work to “read” the painting, making looking at art an active process.

Georges Braque

Georges Braque, Violin and Candlestick 1910
  • Collaborated closely with Picasso in developing Analytic Cubism; their paintings looked very similar in this phase.
  • Favoured still life subjects (musical instruments, bottles), dissecting and reassembling them into complex, overlapping fragments.
  • Emphasised the flatness of the canvas, with overlapping planes and muted colours that blur background and foreground.
  • Used texture, stencilled letters, and trompe-l’œil effects, expanding Cubist possibilities.

Juan Gris

Juan Gris, Still Life with a Guitar 1911
  • Brought clarity, structure and bright, harmonious colour to Cubism.
  • His Cubist works use clearer lines and slightly brighter colours than Picasso or Braque, making them easier to interpret.
  • Bridged the gap between Analytic and Synthetic Cubism, influencing the movement’s next phase.

Fernand Léger

Fernand Léger, The Woman in Blue 1912
  • Created a mechanical, dynamic version of Cubism, using bold cylindrical forms and strong contrasts.
  • Paintings often reflected modern life: machines, cities, workers, and technology.
  • Léger's style (sometimes called “Tubism”) influenced later movements such as Purism and modern graphic design.

Robert Delaunay

Robert Delaunay, Tour Eiffel 1926
  • Developed a colourful, abstract form of Cubism called Orphism, focusing on light and colour.
  • Explored fragmentation, rhythm, and movement in art, expanding Cubism into pure abstraction.
  • His series on the Eiffel Tower exemplifies his vibrant, dynamic approach.

Albert Gleizes

Albert Gleizes, Penelope 1911
  • Important painter and Cubist theorist who helped define and spread Cubist ideas through his writing and teaching.
  • Works feature large, interlocking planes and rhythmic movement.
  • Believed Cubism should reflect modern, collective experience, not individual perception.

Jean Metzinger

Jean Metzinger, Woman with a Horse 1912
  • Developed the theoretical foundations of Cubism with Gleizes, co-authoring the book Du Cubisme.
  • Paintings combine multiple perspectives, fractured forms, and refined, decorative colour and pattern.
  • Helped open Cubism to wider acceptance and understanding.

Marcel Duchamp

Marcel Duchamp, Nude Descending a Staircase, No. 2 1912
  • His Cubist works introduced movement and time as key elements in painting.
  • Blended Cubism with Futurist ideas and questioned what art could be.
  • Paved the way for Dada, conceptual art, and the avant-garde.

Key Themes and Artistic Aspects

  • Multiple viewpoints: Objects are broken down into geometric shapes and shown from different angles at once (like seeing through a shattered mirror).
  • Limited colour palette: Mostly browns, greys, and ochres, to focus attention on form and structure.
  • Active viewing: Paintings are complex and detailed, encouraging close looking and interpretation rather than immediate recognition.
  • Emphasis on flatness: Overlapping planes and subtle colours blur the line between background and foreground, making viewers aware of the two-dimensional surface.
  • Fragmentation: Forms are dissected and reconstructed in abstracted, busy compositions.

Analytic Cubism: In Simple Terms for Students

  • Breaks down objects into parts and shows them from many angles at once, like seeing something through a shattered mirror.
  • Artists used dull colours so you focus on the shapes, lines, and structure, not on bright colours.
  • The paintings often look busy or complicated, especially at the centre, so you have to look closely and think about what you see.

Related Writers and Composers

Writers

  • Guillaume Apollinaire — Poet and Cubism supporter.

Composers

  • Erik Satie, Igor Stravinsky — Experimented with new forms and structures, similar to Cubists in art.
