Feminist Art (1955–1990)

Overview

Feminist Art emerged to confront gender stereotypes and challenge the exclusion of women from art history. Artists addressed women's experiences, used new materials, practised activism, critiqued institutions, and fostered community. This movement influenced wider debates about identity, power, and equality in culture and society.

Three Short Points

  • Challenged gender stereotypes and made women’s lives and bodies visible in art.
  • Experimented with diverse materials and questioned what counts as “real” art.
  • Practised activism—protested sexism, formed collectives, and critiqued art institutions.

Key Themes

Challenging Gender Stereotypes

  • Artists such as Judy Chicago, Barbara Kruger, and the Guerrilla Girls exposed and opposed the way women were depicted as objects or stereotypes in art and society.
  • Celebrated real women’s achievements and experiences, often using self-portraiture and autobiographical content.
  • Sought to rewrite art history from a female perspective and ensure women’s visibility.

Experimenting with Materials

  • Artists used performance, video, photography, textiles, embroidery, and installations—materials often dismissed as “women’s work.”
  • Redefined what is considered art; for instance, Judy Chicago’s The Dinner Party honoured women through ceramics and embroidery, and Eva Hesse challenged Minimalism with latex and rope.
  • Saw mediums as a statement of identity and as a way to break boundaries.

Activism and Critique

  • Formed collectives (like the Guerrilla Girls), protested against sexism, and demanded fair treatment and opportunities for women artists.
  • Campaigned for wider social change—addressing violence against women and creating supportive spaces for marginalised groups.
  • Laid foundations for later explorations of identity, race, and sexuality in art.

Context and Influence

Main Locations

  • USA (especially New York and Los Angeles)
  • United Kingdom
  • Western Europe

Earlier Movements

  • Surrealism
  • Dada (experimentation & performance)
  • Conceptual Art
  • Minimalism (feminist art reacted against their male dominance and lack of personal content)

Culture and Politics

  • Second-wave feminism (1960s–80s)
  • Civil rights, anti-war, and LGBTQ+ movements
  • Activism for women’s equality in all areas of life

Mass Media and Technology

  • Growth of video cameras, photocopiers, and new printmaking for art and activism
  • Widespread use of new materials in sculpture and installation (latex, plastics, textiles)
  • Expanding reach of mass media and television

Typical Fashion

  • Men: Jeans, T-shirts, unisex and casual styles
  • Women: Trousers, denim, slogan T-shirts, sometimes androgynous or protest-inspired fashion
  • Both: Dress reflected comfort, practicality, and countercultural or non-traditional values

Writers and Composers

  • Writers: Linda Nochlin (Why Have There Been No Great Women Artists?), Lucy Lippard, Audre Lorde, Bell Hooks
  • Composers: Laurie Anderson (performance and sound), Meredith Monk

Period

  • 1955–1990

Key Artists

Frida Kahlo

Frida Kahlo, Self-portrait with Thorn Necklace and Hummingbird 1940
  • Explored female pain, sexuality, and identity in powerful self-portraits.
  • Challenged gender roles and beauty standards, showing facial hair and addressing taboo subjects.
  • Expressed bisexuality and political beliefs openly; has become an international icon of resilience.
  • Transformed personal experience into universal messages on womanhood and strength.

Hannah Höch

Hannah Höch, Cut with the Kitchen Knife 1919–20
  • Pioneered Dada photomontage, critiquing gender roles and media images of women.
  • Addressed androgyny, diversity, and constructed identity.
  • Set foundations for later feminist art with her experimental style and bold activism.

Eva Hesse

Eva Hesse, Untitled (or Not Yet) 1966
  • Used latex, fibreglass, and rope to defy Minimalism’s masculine norms.
  • Works explored vulnerability, process, and the body—often referencing impermanence and female experience.
  • Influenced generations of feminist and post-minimalist artists through her material innovation.

Yoko Ono

Yoko Ono, Cut Piece 1964
  • Conceptual and performance art addressed female agency, peace, and vulnerability.
  • Created participatory works (“Cut Piece”) challenging audience behaviour and women’s objectification.
  • Blended art, activism, and avant-garde performance.

Judy Chicago

Judy Chicago, The Dinner Party 1974–79
  • Best known for “The Dinner Party”—a collaborative installation honouring women from history.
  • Championed traditionally feminine crafts (ceramics, embroidery) as high art.
  • Promoted collective art-making and feminist arts education.
  • Work foregrounds empowerment, bodily autonomy, and reclaiming silenced histories.

Louise Bourgeois

Louise Bourgeois, Maman 1999
  • Explored motherhood, sexuality, and trauma in expressive sculptures and installations.
  • Borrowed from personal memory to create giant spiders, referencing protection and female power.
  • Works offer profound reflections on identity, the unconscious, and vulnerability.

Nancy Spero

Nancy Spero, Artemis the Huntress, The Artemis Series 1993
  • Combined text and image to reclaim mythologies and histories for women.
  • Tackled topics such as war, oppression, and erasure of women’s voices.
  • Committed to political activism, experimentation, and collective practice.

Nancy Azara

Nancy Azara, Green Heart with Doors 2000
  • Sculptures and installations use natural materials and symbols to explore female spirituality and transformation.
  • Art is informed by consciousness-raising, ritual, and connection with nature.
  • Honours intuition, process, and the body’s relationship to the environment.

Ana Mendieta

Ana Mendieta, Silueta Series 1973
  • Used her own body and natural materials to create “earth-body” works that explored identity, exile, and belonging.
  • Imprinted herself on landscapes, uniting themes of vulnerability, power, and connection with nature.
  • Pioneered intersections of feminist, Latinx, and performance art.

Barbara Kruger

Barbara Kruger, Your body is a battleground 1989
  • Used bold text and imagery to highlight gender, power, and consumer culture.
  • Adopted advertising and media styles to subvert patriarchal messages.
  • Famous for slogans like “Your body is a battleground,” sparking debate on feminism and activism.

Jenny Holzer

Jenny Holzer, Protect Me From What I Want 1986
  • Projected urgent, provocative statements (“Truisms”) on public billboards and electronic screens.
  • Tackled gender, violence, and human rights, making art part of daily urban life.
  • Brought feminist issues to wide audiences through language and space.

Mary Kelly

Mary Kelly, Post-Partum Document 1973–79
  • Analysed motherhood, domestic labour, and femininity through conceptual installation.
  • Used language, documentation, and detailed narrative to reveal the roles and expectations imposed on women.
  • Set new standards for feminist art theory and academic practice.

Mierle Laderman Ukeles

Mierle Laderman Ukeles, Touch Sanitation Performance 1979–80
  • Celebrated maintenance, care, and undervalued labour (especially “women’s work”) as art.
  • “Maintenance Art” elevated everyday acts like cleaning to artistic status.
  • Focused on the connections between art, life, and social value.

Guerrilla Girls

Guerrilla Girls, Do women have to be naked to get into the Met. Museum? 1989
  • Anonymous collective using humour, posters, and statistics to expose sexism and racism in the art world.
  • Famous for their gorilla masks and witty campaigns demanding equality and representation.
  • Brought institutional critique and feminist activism into mainstream art and media.

Helen Chadwick

Helen Chadwick, Ego Geometry Lucy 1982–83
  • Explored female sexuality and the blurred boundaries between public/private life with multimedia installations.
  • Used unusual materials to question conventional beauty and femininity.
  • Renowned for combining intellectual and sensual explorations of the body.

Sherrie Levine

Sherrie Levine, After Walker Evans: 2 1981
  • Appropriated works by male artists to challenge authorship and the male art canon.
  • Asked who gets to be original, whose work is celebrated, and how value is constructed in art.
  • Influential in both postmodern and feminist critique of culture.

Glossary for Students

  • Patriarchy: A social system where men hold more power and authority.
  • Institutional critique: Art that examines rules, power, and structures in the art world.
  • Performance art: Art that uses the artist’s body or actions as the artwork.
  • Textiles: Woven or sewn fabrics and materials used in art, often linked to craft.
  • Activism: Taking action to bring about social or political change.
