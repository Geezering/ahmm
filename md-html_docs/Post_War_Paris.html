Post-War Paris

Key Themes, Ideas, and Artistic Aspects

Existentialism and the Search for Meaning

  • After World War II, many Parisian artists were strongly influenced by existentialist philosophy, exploring the search for meaning and authenticity in a world changed by suffering and upheaval.
  • Their art often expressed themes of anxiety, personal freedom, and the cultural and personal challenge of rebuilding.

Abstract and Lyrical Painting

  • Artists moved away from realistic depiction towards abstraction and “lyrical abstraction”—a style marked by expressive brushwork, spontaneous gesture, and vibrant colour.
  • Painters such as Pierre Soulages and Nicolas de Staël experimented with new ways to convey emotion, balancing figuration (recognisable images) with pure abstraction.

Art Brut and New Materials

  • Jean Dubuffet pioneered “Art Brut” (“raw art”), championing the creativity of outsiders and using rough textures and unconventional materials.
  • Many artists broke traditional boundaries, experimenting with new tools, media, and ways of integrating art with daily life—reflecting the urge to reinvent art in a changed post-war world.

Contextual Information

Main Locations

  • Paris, France (the epicentre of post-war artistic innovation)

Relevant Previous Art Movements

  • Surrealism
  • Analytic Cubism and Synthetic Cubism
  • Pre-war Abstraction
  • War’s disruption led to a rejection of old certainties in art and culture

Relevant Culture and Politics

  • Post-war reconstruction efforts; existentialist philosophy (Jean-Paul Sartre, Albert Camus); a cultural drive to rebuild France
  • Paris regained global influence, drawing international artists and serving as a hub of creativity

Contemporary Inventions and Technology

  • New art materials and techniques (synthetic paints, collage, assemblage)
  • Growth in mass media, photography, and early television

Typical Fashions of the Day

  • Men: Tailored suits, berets, trench coats, and practical post-war styles
  • Women: "New Look" by Dior (nipped waists, full skirts), elegant hats, and a return to glamour after wartime austerity

Key Artists

Jean Dubuffet

Jean Dubuffet, Grand Messager 1952

"Grand Messager" is a work by French artist Jean Dubuffet, created in 1961. It is part of his larger body of work exploring "art brut" or "outsider art," which emphasizes raw, uninhibited expression. "Grand Messager" is rendered in vivid colors and larger-than-life dimensions, drawing the viewer into a unique topography of characters and forms. The piece is a testament to Dubuffet's revolutionary approach to art, his interest in the tactile possibilities of materials, and his fascination with the human form.

  • French painter and sculptor; founder of Art Brut (“raw art”), championing outsider creativity over academic tradition.
  • Used unconventional materials such as tar, gravel, sand, and found objects to create rough, textured works.
  • His writings and radical techniques inspired later artists to challenge cultural values and seek new forms of authentic, instinctive expression.

Pierre Soulages

Pierre Soulages, Peinture 222x226 2009
  • French abstract painter, famous for his exploration of black within “outrenoir” (“beyond black”), finding light and depth in darkness.
  • Known for thick, gestural brushstrokes and textured surfaces reflecting and absorbing light.
  • Soulages’s radical minimalism and material focus made him a giant in postwar and contemporary art.

Nicolas de Staël

Nicolas de Staël, Le Fort Carré d'Antibes 1955
  • Russian-born French painter; known for luminous, abstract landscapes and still lifes, characterised by blocks of bold colour and thick paint.
  • Bridged figuration and abstraction, using colour, composition, and impasto to evoke atmosphere and emotion.
  • Influential in European painting for expressive surfaces and poetic sensibility.

Hans Hartung

Hans Hartung, At work 1963

In 1963, Hans Hartung was actively creating gestural, abstract paintings that were part of the Art Informel movement. He was known for his expressive and dynamic compositions, often using bold, calligraphic brushstrokes and experimenting with techniques like rakes and olive branches. Some of his works from this period are titled "P1963 - 8" and "T 1963-K17". Hartung's work was gaining recognition internationally, with exhibitions in major cities like New York, Paris, and London.

  • German-French painter, pioneer of gestural abstraction (“Tachisme”).
  • Created energetic, calligraphic works using spontaneous marks and unconventional tools.
  • Influenced Art Informel and Abstract Expressionism in Europe.

Jean Fautrier

Jean Fautrier, Dépouille 1945

In 1943, while producing his Otages (Hostages) series, Jean Fautrier hid from the Gestapo in a psychiatric asylum outside Paris. At night he heard gunfire in the surrounding woods as Nazi authorities massacred French civilians. Given this painting’s lurid title, Dépouille, meaning “skin” or “human remains,” and the horrific context of the series to which it belongs, viewers might be surprised to encounter a nebulous abstract form and an appealing, frothy palette. However, what this signals is a conundrum: how should a visual artist address Nazi atrocities without sensationalizing them? Fautrier’s strategy was to indicate violence in his handling of materials, rather than through traditional figurative means. He created Dépouille by smearing a stuccolike white paste across a layered paper surface, then sprinkling pastel powders on the central shape, loosely delineated in blue brushstrokes. It is the painting’s rough, scored, cracked matter above all that portrays the brutalization of bodies.

  • French painter and sculptor associated with Art Informel and Tachisme, known for his focus on texture and psychological depth.
  • His “Hostages” series, created during WWII, used thick layers of paint to evoke suffering and trauma.
  • Helped define postwar European art’s emphasis on materiality and emotion.

Bram van Velde

Bram van Velde, Untitled (Paris Rue de la Coeur) 1962

In 1967, Bram van Velde, the Dutch abstract painter, was officially living in Geneva, Switzerland. He had been dividing his time between Paris and Geneva, but made the move to Geneva permanent in 1967. 1967 also saw him undertake a lithographic production that would eventually include four hundred pieces. A specific artwork from this period is "Sans titre, Carouge" , 1967

  • Dutch painter associated with the School of Paris and Abstract Expressionism.
  • His paintings are known for sombre palettes, enigmatic forms, and a mood of melancholy and introspection.
  • Admired by writers such as Samuel Beckett for poetic ambiguity and emotional depth.

Pablo Picasso

Pablo Picasso, Mousquetaire 1947
  • Spanish artist, a founder of Analytic Cubism; one of the most influential figures in 20th-century art.
  • His evolving style embraced Synthetic Cubism, Neoclassicism, Surrealism, and post-war innovation.
  • Masterpieces like “Guernica” and “Les Demoiselles d’Avignon” are icons of Western art.

Henri Matisse

Henri Matisse, The Lute 1943
  • French artist and leader of Fauvism, known for bold colour, expressive line, and decorative pattern.
  • Later works (cut-outs) balanced colour and form in new, monumental compositions.
  • Rival to Picasso, shaping the direction of modern art pre- and post-war.

Fernand Léger

Fernand Léger, La Grande Julienne 1951–52
  • French artist known for bold, modern style inspired by Cubism and Purism.
  • His works celebrated technology and urban life with tubular forms and rhythmic, colourful compositions.
  • Léger’s art bridged abstraction and figuration, influencing public art, murals, mosaics, and film.

Three Short Points for Students

  • Post-war Paris artists explored deep questions about existence and meaning, shaped by war trauma and existentialism.
  • They invented new forms of expressive, sometimes abstract art, often mixing painting with new materials and gestures.
  • Paris became the centre for creative rebuilding and innovation in art after WWII.

Glossary for Students

  • Existentialism: A philosophy about finding meaning and making choices in a world without clear answers.
  • Abstraction: Art that uses shapes, colours, and textures to express ideas and feelings, rather than recognisable objects.
  • Lyrical abstraction: Expressive, emotional, and often spontaneous abstract painting.
  • Art Brut: “Raw art” created outside traditional art circles, often by self-taught artists using unusual materials.
