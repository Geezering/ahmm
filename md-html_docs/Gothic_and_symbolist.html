Gothic & Symbolist Art (c. 1860–1920)

Gothic and Symbolist art emphasised imagination, dreams, and hidden meanings over everyday reality. Artists explored emotion, spirituality, and the mysteries of the mind, often using moody colours, mythological subjects, and complex symbols.

Key Themes & Ideas

Dreams, Mysticism & Imagination

  • Artists expressed emotions, dreams, and visions from the subconscious rather than painting the visible world as it appears.
  • Works are filled with mystical and religious themes, mythological stories, and imaginative symbolism.
  • Symbolic images stand for inner feelings and spiritual truths.

Atmosphere, Mood & Colour

  • Symbolist painters created mysterious, dreamlike moods with soft, glowing colours and unusual lighting.
  • Artworks often feel melancholic, eerie, or mysterious, with strange settings and figures.
  • Colour is used to suggest emotion and guide what viewers feel.

Personal Expression & Hidden Meanings

  • Focused on individual ideas and emotions (subjectivity), often including secret or esoteric symbols and references to myths, magic, and the supernatural.
  • Art explores universal themes—love, death, sin, desire—through metaphor and storytelling.
  • Viewers are encouraged to interpret and find their own meaning in the works.

Contextual Information

Main Locations

  • France and Belgium (origins), later in Austria, Germany, Russia, and elsewhere in Europe.

Relevant Previous Movements

  • Gothic art (spiritual, mystical detail, and visual symbolism)
  • Romanticism (emotion, imagination, the supernatural)
  • Realism/Naturalism (Symbolism was a reaction against focus on reality)

Culture & Politics

  • Late 19th-century Europe: intense social change, new scientific ideas, and the growth of industrial, modern life.
  • Artists reacted against materialism and rational thinking by exploring dreams and the spiritual dimension.
  • New theories of the subconscious (Freud) inspired artists to look inward.

Relevant Writers & Composers

Writers

  • Charles Baudelaire
  • Stéphane Mallarmé
  • Paul Verlaine (poets using symbol and mood)

Composers

  • Claude Debussy
  • Richard Wagner (music built on atmosphere and emotion)

Inventions & Technology

  • Advances in printmaking and book illustration (e.g., Gustave Doré)
  • Photography, which changed how artists saw composition and light
  • Electric lighting (created new effects in theatre and art)

Typical Fashion

  • Men: Tailcoats, waistcoats, cravats, top hats.
  • Women: Corseted dresses, long skirts, elaborate hats, rich fabrics and flowing, decorative details inspired by history and fantasy.

Key Artists & the Symbolist Movement

  • Period: Late 19th to early 20th century (c. 1860–1920 for Symbolism; Gothic revival influences earlier and ongoing)

Henry Fuseli

Henry Fuseli, The Nightmare 1781
  • Renowned for dramatic depictions of dreams, the supernatural, and psychological tension.
  • Famous for mysterious, symbolic scenes like The Nightmare.
  • Precursor to Symbolism and Surrealism, exploring the unconscious and irrational.

Gustave Doré

Gustave Doré, Inferno (Dante illustration) 1857
  • Master illustrator, produced dramatic, detailed engravings for works like Dante’s Divine Comedy and Paradise Lost.
  • Used epic scale and mysterious light to evoke awe, horror, and fantasy.
  • His visionary images influenced later Symbolist painters and poets.

Odilon Redon

Odilon Redon, The Cyclops c. 1914
  • Created dreamlike, mysterious artworks using bold colours and fantastic subjects.
  • Explored mythology, literature, and his own imagination to evoke spiritual longing and emotion.
  • A central figure in Symbolism, celebrated for his unique, poetic style.

Pierre Puvis de Chavannes

Pierre Puvis de Chavannes, Faith in God 1883
  • Known for calm, mural-like paintings that suggest mood and timelessness.
  • Used simple shapes and soft colours to evoke reflection and poetic atmosphere.
  • Influenced Symbolists through his focus on mood over realism.

Henri Fantin-Latour

Henri Fantin-Latour, Self-Portrait 1859
  • Known for imaginative, poetic prints and group portraits of artists and poets.
  • Blended allegory, myth, and music with delicate colour and light.
  • Bridged Realism and Symbolism with a focus on fantasy and mood.

Gustave Moreau

Gustave Moreau, Oedipus and the Sphinx 1864
  • Specialist in mythological and biblical symbolism, famous for intricate detail and glowing colour.
  • Paintings are filled with allegory, mystery, and imaginative vision.
  • Mentored a new generation of Symbolist artists.

Gustav Klimt

Gustav Klimt, The Kiss 1907–08
  • Renowned for decorative, symbolic images, sensuous portraits, and use of gold leaf.
  • Explored love, death, nature, and regeneration with patterns and symbolic motifs.
  • Merged the sensual and the spiritual in his unique visual language.

Frida Kahlo

Frida Kahlo, Self-portrait with Thorn Necklace and Hummingbird 1940
  • Painted deeply personal, symbolic self-portraits exploring pain, heritage, resilience, and transformation.
  • Combined elements from Mexican tradition and mythology with psychological insight.
  • Her inner focus and use of fantasy connects her to the Symbolist movement.

Eleuna Gorokhova

Eleuna Gorokhova, The Butterfly of the Rainbow 1912
  • Keen on poetic, mystical themes drawn from Russian folklore.
  • Used delicate lines and subtle colour for a mood of introspection and mystery.
  • Her work highlights Symbolist focus on imagination and the unseen.

Auguste Rodin

Auguste Rodin, The Thinker c. 1902
  • Created sculptures like The Thinker and The Gates of Hell with Symbolist themes of struggle and desire.
  • His expressive modelling captures emotion, the psyche, and existential uncertainty.
  • Inspired Symbolists—and later modernists—with bold portrayals of inner experience.

Glossary for Students

  • Symbolism: Using images or objects to stand for deeper ideas or emotions.
  • Mysticism: Belief in spiritual or hidden truths beyond normal understanding.
  • Esoteric: Secret, mysterious, or known only to a few.
  • Subjectivity: Personal feelings and experiences matter most.
  • Metaphor: Describing something as if it is something else, to create a deeper meaning.
