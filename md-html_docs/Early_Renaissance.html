Early Renaissance (1450–1550)

The Early Renaissance was a period of exciting change in art, society, and culture, mainly centred in Italy. Artists began to focus on making their work look realistic, emotional, and alive.

Where Did It Happen?

  • Florence, along with other central Italian cities, was the centre of the Early Renaissance.
  • Florence became famous for artistic progress, boosted by wealthy patrons such as the Medici family [8].

What Came Before?

  • Gothic and Romanesque art: characteristically flat, stylised, and mainly religious.
  • Proto-Renaissance: Artists like Giotto and Duccio started moving towards naturalism and three-dimensional art in the 1300s [1].

Culture and Politics

  • Rising interest in Humanism: celebrating human potential, individuality, and classical antiquity.
  • Wealthy merchant families, notably the Medici, supported artists [8].
  • Curiosity about science, maths, and anatomy inspired new artistic approaches [5].

Writers and Composers

Writers

  • Dante Alighieri
  • Petrarch & Boccaccio – early Humanist writers
  • Leon Battista Alberti – art theorist

Composers

  • Guillaume Dufay
  • Josquin des Prez – led changes in sacred and secular music

Inventions and Technology

  • Mathematical perspective: creating depth on flat surfaces [4].
  • Anatomical studies: leading to more lifelike figures [5].
  • New painting techniques: oil painting, fresco, and tempera innovations [4].
  • The mid-15th century printing press rapidly spread fresh ideas.

Typical Fashions

  • Men: Doublets, hose, tunics, and hats or caps.
  • Women: Long, fitted dresses, high waists, decorated sleeves, and hair in braids or nets.

Key Artists

These artists shaped the Early Renaissance by experimenting with realism, perspective, and emotion.

Giotto

Giotto, Kiss of Judas 1305
  • Broke away from medieval traditions with lifelike, three-dimensional figures.
  • Used convincing emotion and storytelling in works such as the Scrovegni Chapel frescoes [1].
  • Helped create the illusion of real space, bringing stories to life.
  • Focused on natural gestures and simple, powerful layouts.
  • Influenced generations of later artists.
  1. Made realistic, emotional figures.
  2. Used shading and depth unlike anyone before.
  3. Told stories clearly and dramatically.

Duccio

Duccio, Maestà (detail) 1308–1311
  • Bridged the gap from Gothic to Renaissance art with softer, more natural lines.
  • Painted large altarpieces out of small, story-telling panels.
  • Used delicate patterns and rich colours for beauty as well as narrative.
  • Foreshadowed the move to realism in art.
  • Influenced the direction of Italian painting.
  1. Linked Gothic and Renaissance art.
  2. Specialised in narrative panel paintings.
  3. Brought gentle realism and beautiful colour.

Masaccio

Masaccio, The Holy Trinity 1427–1428
  • Pioneered the use of mathematical (linear) perspective for realistic depth [4].
  • Painted with accurate human anatomy and dramatic lighting (chiaroscuro).
  • Brought emotion and lifelike quality to religious subjects.
  • Frescoes in the Brancacci Chapel are still studied today.
  • Inspired a new generation of artists.
  1. Brought depth and perspective to painting.
  2. Painted true-to-life people.
  3. Mixed drama and honest feeling in his work.

Paolo Uccello

Paolo Uccello, The Battle of San Romano c. 1435
  • Fascinated by geometry and perspective—his paintings have dramatic, precise spaces.
  • Blended Gothic detail with Renaissance ideas of space and form.
  • Created energetic, almost puzzle-like compositions using foreshortening.
  • Best known for "The Battle of San Romano".
  • Showed how maths could make art more exciting.
  1. Pushed maths and perspective in art.
  2. Combined decorative detail with realism.
  3. Made bold, action-packed compositions.

Key Themes and Ideas

  • Artists aimed for realism: using maths and science to create depth and lifelike bodies.
  • Inspired by classical (Greek and Roman) art but wanted to show real people and everyday life.
  • Their work was emotional, dramatic, and brought new meaning to art.

In Simple Terms

  • Early Renaissance art looks real – figures, space, and emotions all come alive.
  • Inspired by the world around them as well as religious stories.
  • Started an artistic revolution that changed everything.

Why Does This Matter?

  • This period set the foundations for all later Western art—their drive for realism and emotion made their work timeless [1].
