YBA (Young British Artists)

Overview

The Young British Artists (YBAs) are a group of artists from late 1980s and 1990s London, known for their provocative, concept-driven, and media-savvy approach. They challenged traditional ideas of art through shock tactics, use of everyday materials, and personal and cultural commentary—shaping the era of "Cool Britannia."

Key Themes and Ideas

Shock and Provocation

  • YBA artists often used controversial materials and taboo subjects to shock and provoke debate.
  • Examples include Damien Hirst’s animals in formaldehyde and Tracey Emin’s deeply confessional installations.
  • The aim was to question mainstream expectations, force reflection, and spark public discussion about what art can be.

Everyday Materials and Conceptual Focus

  • YBAs used everyday items—beds, food, rubbish, furniture—and industrial objects rather than traditional paint or marble.
  • This shifted focus from what art looks like to the ideas and questions it raises.
  • Sarah Lucas, for example, used food and furniture to comment on gender and identity, while many artists explored the meaning and value of art itself.

Personal and Cultural Commentary

  • Many works are autobiographical or investigate social issues such as identity, mortality, consumerism, and the effects of mass media.
  • Personal experience often blends with broader cultural critique, as in Tracey Emin’s My Bed or Gillian Wearing’s photographs of anonymous confessions.
  • This approach made YBA art both relatable and challenging, blurring the boundaries between personal story and social commentary.

Context and Development

Main Locations

  • London, especially the East End
  • Many artists studied at Goldsmiths College and showed in unconventional venues like warehouses and factories

Influences and Previous Movements

  • Expressionism.html (intensely peronalised subject matter and forms of representation)
  • DaDa (Dislocated and temporary performances and installations that provoke, annoy and outrage)
  • Performance Art (Theatrical events and installations)
  • Minimalism (stripped-down forms and materials)
  • Arte Povera (stripped-down forms and materials)
  • Conceptual Art (focus on ideas)

Culture and Politics

  • 1990s Britain—time of rapid economic change, “Cool Britannia,” and debates about identity, globalisation, and media influence
  • Artworks reflected both the optimism and anxieties of this era

Relevant Writers and Musicians

  • Writers: Martin Amis, Irvine Welsh (known for rebellious, gritty style)
  • Music: Britpop (Blur, Oasis); critics: Sarah Kent, Matthew Collings

Technology and Materials

  • The internet and mobile phones became widespread in the 1990s
  • Mass media, advertising, and new forms of dissemination became central to art and culture

Typical Fashion

  • Men: Baggy jeans, graphic T-shirts, trainers, sportswear
  • Women: Slip dresses, platform shoes, crop tops, "heroin chic" (minimal make-up, skinny looks)
  • Both: Vintage/second-hand style reflecting rebellion and anti-establishment attitude

Period

  • 1988–1999 (core years; YBA influence continues today)

Key Artists

Damien Hirst

Damien Hirst, The Physical Impossibility of Death in the Mind of Someone Living 1991
  • Central figure of the YBAs, famous for installations using preserved animals (e.g. a shark in formaldehyde), medicine cabinets, and butterfly paintings.
  • Explores life, death, beauty, and the fragility of existence using commercial and medical imagery.
  • Curator of the pivotal “Freeze” exhibition; challenged the boundaries of what art could be.
  • Work is both celebrated and controversial for its shock value and media impact.

Sarah Lucas

Sarah Lucas, Au Naturel 1994
  • Known for irreverent sculptures, photos, and installations using everyday objects (such as furniture, food, cigarettes) to comment on gender and sexuality.
  • Uses humour and visual puns to challenge social conventions and explore taboo topics.
  • Part of the YBAs’ core group, her work is direct, witty, and confrontational.

Tracey Emin

Tracey Emin, My Bed 1998
  • Champion of confessional and autobiographical art: installations, neon texts, embroidery, and drawing.
  • My Bed and Everyone I Have Ever Slept With made vulnerability and raw personal experience central to contemporary art.
  • Her work is emotional, direct, and both acclaimed and controversial.

Gillian Wearing

Gillian Wearing, Signs that say what you want them to say... 1992–93
  • Known for participatory photography and video projects investigating identity and individual voice.
  • Projects like "Signs that say what you want them to say..." invited strangers to reveal private thoughts on camera.
  • Work blurs boundaries between public and private, focusing on psychological depth.

Gavin Turk

Gavin Turk, Bum 1998
  • Questions authenticity and authorship through sculpture, installation, and appropriation of historic styles.
  • Known for self-portraits and works referencing art icons, including his own inclusion/exclusion from mainstream art history.
  • His conceptual approach is critical, witty, and central to YBA debates on identity and originality.

Jake and Dinos Chapman

Jake and Dinos Chapman, Hell 2000
  • Brothers known for provocative, often grotesque sculptures and installations commenting on violence, history, and pop culture.
  • Use shock and meticulous detail to explore the limits of artistic and moral boundaries.
  • Work sparks debate about the responsibilities and possibilities of art.

Rachel Whiteread

Rachel Whiteread, House 1993
  • Sculptor best known for casting the negative spaces of everyday forms using materials like plaster, resin, and concrete.
  • Famous works include House—a full-size cast of the interior of a Victorian house—and Mattress.
  • Work explores memory, absence, and traces of human experience; winner of the Turner Prize.

Marcus Harvey

Marcus Harvey, Myra 1995
  • Gained attention for “Myra”—a portrait of serial killer Myra Hindley assembled from children's handprints—provoking controversy and debate.
  • Work explores celebrity, infamy, and darker sides of culture.
  • Takes a provocative approach to the moral impact of art.

Michael Landy

Michael Landy, Break Down 2001
  • Best known for “Break Down”—destroying all his possessions as a public performance.
  • His work critiques consumerism, value, and what defines identity in contemporary society.
  • Engages with ideas of transformation, destruction, and the meaning of material life.

Chris Ofili

Chris Ofili, The Holy Virgin Mary 1996
  • Painter famous for vibrant, detailed works that use unconventional materials, including glitter and elephant dung.
  • Explores African culture, race, identity, and spirituality through layered symbolism.
  • His art won the Turner Prize and challenged traditional ideas of beauty and value.

Marc Quinn

Marc Quinn, Self 1991
  • Known for “Self”—sculptures of his head made from his own frozen blood—which explore identity, mortality, and the body.
  • His work addresses themes of transformation, disability, and the boundaries of selfhood.
  • Raises questions about permanence and fragility in art and life.

Yinka Shonibare

Yinka Shonibare, Mr and Mrs Andrews without their Heads 1998
  • British-Nigerian artist using Dutch wax fabrics to reference African culture and European art history.
  • Multimedia works question colonialism, identity, and globalisation.
  • Work is visually striking, playful, and deep in its exploration of cultural hybridity.

Glenn Brown

Glenn Brown, The Life Hereafter 2004
  • Known for meticulously painted, surreal works that appropriate and reinvent images from art history and pop culture.
  • Paintings are often distorted, glossy, and highly detailed—blurring real and digital appearances.
  • Raises questions about originality, representation, and the nature of painting today.

Mark Wallinger

Mark Wallinger, State Britain 2007
  • Artist known for conceptual works exploring identity, politics, religion, and public space.
  • Turner Prize winner for State Britain (recreating a peace protest removed from Parliament Square).
  • Work has a strong social dimension, often engaging directly with British life and political debate.

BANK (Artist Collective)

BANK Collective, Zombie Golf 1994
  • Satirical London-based collective active through the 1990s.
  • Critiqued art market commercialisation and institutional elitism through irreverent group exhibitions and publications.
  • Exposed mechanisms of power and value in the art world, challenging assumptions and authority.
  • Full page on BANK

Three Short Points for Students

  • YBAs used shock tactics and everyday materials to challenge art’s limits and make viewers question their own beliefs and society’s values.
  • Their art was personal, often controversial, and responded to changing British identity, media, and consumer culture during the 1990s.
  • The movement helped turn artists into celebrities, blurring the lines between art, entrepreneurship, and popular media.

Glossary for Students

  • Conceptual art: Art where the main emphasis is on the idea and concept, not traditional skills or finished objects.
  • Consumerism: A society focused on buying, owning, and displaying things.
  • Taboo: Subjects considered shocking or off-limits in polite society.
  • The YBAs made art that was bold, raw, and relevant to their era, leaving a permanent mark on contemporary art.
