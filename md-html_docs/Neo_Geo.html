Neo Geo

Overview

Neo Geo (short for Neo-Geometric Conceptualism) is an art movement from the 1980s known for its sharp, geometric abstraction and critical stance on consumer society and technology. Artists used clean lines, industrial materials, and irony to explore themes of mass production, artificiality, and the blurred line between art and consumer goods.

Key Themes and Ideas

Geometric Abstraction as Social Critique

  • Artists employed geometric shapes and abstract forms to reflect and question the rigid, artificial structures of city and modern life.
  • Their art highlights the effects of consumerism, commercialisation, and technology on society, often depicting feelings of isolation or alienation.
  • Geometry in their work stands for both order and emptiness in a rapidly changing, digital age.

Irony, Simulation, and Mass Production

  • Neo Geo artists created work that intentionally looked synthetic or mass-produced, often blurring distinctions between fine art and branded commodities.
  • They borrowed visual elements from advertising and industrial design, turning familiar images and logos into artworks.
  • Work often played with the irony that art meant to critique consumerism could itself become fashionable and expensive.

Ambiguity and Self-Reflection

  • Many Neo Geo pieces are self-aware, questioning whether true artistic critique is possible when art is itself part of the market.
  • Artists used bright colours, shiny surfaces, and familiar motifs to attract viewers while also making them reflect on the meaning and value of art in a world of reproductions.
  • This movement highlights the contradictions of postmodern life, where art can be both a critique and a product.

Context and Development

Main Locations

  • New York City (especially the East Village art scene)
  • Other major Western cities

Influences and Previous Movements

  • Minimalism (simple geometric abstraction)
  • Pop Art (use of popular imagery and consumer references)
  • Op Art (optical effects, pattern)
  • Conceptual Art (idea-based approaches, questioning tradition)

Culture and Politics

  • 1980s: Rapid expansion of consumer culture, globalisation, widespread technology use
  • Artists consciously critiqued capitalism and the booming art market, yet also participated in it
  • Philosophical influence from postmodern thinkers, especially Jean Baudrillard (ideas about reality, simulation, copying)

Writers and Composers

  • Writers/Philosophers: Jean Baudrillard, Tricia Collins & Richard Milazzo (curators/critics)
  • Composers: Philip Glass, Steve Reich (minimalism and repetitive geometric patterns)

Technology and Materials

  • Widespread mass production and industrial design
  • Synthetic materials and new industrial fabrication techniques
  • Growth of computers, digital graphics, and new printing methods

Typical Fashions of the Era

  • Men and Women: Bold, geometric prints; power suits; designer labels; high-tech, pop-culture inspired looks

Key Artists

Peter Halley

Peter Halley, Exploding Cell 1990
  • Known for paintings of prisons, cells, grids, and “conduits” using fluorescent colour and flat, industrial textures.
  • His geometric style critiques social systems, urban life, and isolation in the technological age.
  • Halley’s writing and art both reflect themes of control, repetition, and the artificial nature of modern environments.

Jeff Koons

Jeff Koons, Balloon Dog (Magenta) 1994–2000
  • Creates artworks based on everyday consumer objects, toys, and advertising images, employing industrial fabrication and shiny, flawless surfaces.
  • His work oscillates between critique and celebration of consumer culture and mass production.
  • Known for using irony and spectacle, Koons raises questions about originality, value, and the “high” and “low” in art.

Ashley Bickerton

Ashley Bickerton, Seascape: Floating Costume to Drift for Eternity II 1992
  • Famous for combining painting, sculpture, and industrial labels to satirise branding and consumerism.
  • Works feature corporate icons, barcode-like motifs, and shiny, artificial finishes.
  • Bickerton’s art explores identity, simulation, and the impact of mass media.

Allan McCollum

Allan McCollum, Collection of Seventy-Five Eight-by-Ten-Inch Drawings 1991
  • Creates serial, mass-produced installations—thousands of objects that are similar but never identical.
  • Questions uniqueness, repetition, and value in the art market, echoing assembly-line production.
  • Work suggests tension between individuality and mass culture.

Haim Steinbach

Haim Steinbach, supreme 2013
  • Famed for presenting everyday consumer items (e.g. detergent boxes, trainers) on custom-made shelves.
  • Investigates how context and presentation affect the value of objects, blurring lines between art and product.
  • Work highlights the power of branding and the aesthetics of display in modern culture.

Three Short Points for Students

  • Neo Geo art uses geometric styles and irony to critique consumer culture, technology, and mass production.
  • Artists mimic advertising, create works that look manufactured, and question their own place in the art market.
  • Neo Geo highlights how art can be both a criticism and a product—raising questions about originality, value, and meaning today.

Glossary for Students

  • Geometric abstraction: Art using shapes like squares, circles, and grids, often without direct reference to real objects.
  • Simulation: Imitation or reproduction of something, often so well made that it’s hard to tell from the “real” thing.
  • Consumerism: Focus on buying and owning goods; a major theme in Neo Geo art.
  • Ambiguity: Something that can be understood in more than one way or doesn’t have a clear single meaning.
