Pre-Raphaelites (1848–1870)

The Pre-Raphaelites were a group of artists and writers in Victorian England who aimed to make art more sincere, vibrant, and truthful. They championed careful observation of nature, vivid stories from legend and literature, and a return to medieval inspiration.

Key Themes & Ideas

Naturalism & Vivid Detail

  • Pre-Raphaelite artists aimed for striking realism and sharp clarity, closely studying the natural world.
  • Their paintings have intense colours and show minute details—each leaf, flower, or hair is painted with care so the scene looks almost photographic.

Medievalism, Symbolism & Storytelling

  • Inspired by medieval art and poetry, they painted scenes from myth, legend, and literature.
  • Objects and settings are symbolic—a flower might represent love or sorrow, and every detail carries meaning.

Rebellion Against Academic Art & Industrialisation

  • The group rejected the set styles of the Royal Academy, preferring direct, emotional, and sincere subjects.
  • They often reacted against the effects of industrialisation, instead showing an unspoilt, beautiful, and spiritual world.

Contextual Information

Main Locations

  • London and cities across England

Previous Art Movements

  • Romanticism (for emotion and nature)
  • Early Renaissance and medieval art (for bright colour and narrative)
  • Against Neoclassicism and Royal Academy rules

Culture & Politics

  • Victorian Britain: rapid change, industrialisation, social transformation, and debates about religion and morality.
  • Concerns about lost beauty and spirituality in a modernising society.

Relevant Writers & Composers

Writers

  • John Ruskin (art critic and supporter)
  • Alfred Lord Tennyson, John Keats, Dante Alighieri, William Shakespeare

Composers

  • Felix Mendelssohn—his music’s poetic spirit matched the group’s love of legend and literature.

Contemporary Inventions & Technology

  • Early photography (influenced their attention to detail).
  • Mass production and printing techniques (which they often criticised).

Typical Fashion

  • Men: Frock coats, waistcoats, cravats, top hats.
  • Women: Full skirts, corsets, bonnets. Later, looser medieval-style dresses and flowing hair (inspired by Pre-Raphaelite models) influenced wider fashion.

Key Artists & the Brotherhood

  • Active years: 1848–1870 (though ideas continued later)

William Holman Hunt

William Holman Hunt, The Light of the World 1851–1853
  • Founder member, famous for his sharp detail, glowing colour, and inventive symbolism.
  • Believed art should reveal both visible reality and moral truth.
  • His most famous works are deeply religious and literary in theme.

John Everett Millais

John Everett Millais, Ophelia 1851–1852
  • Co-founder, known for luminous colour, technical skill, and psychological insight.
  • Ophelia (from Shakespeare) is iconic for its naturalism and emotional atmosphere.
  • Later became a respected portrait painter and President of the Royal Academy.

Dante Gabriel Rossetti

Dante Gabriel Rossetti, Beata Beatrix 1864–1870
  • Founding member and poet—famous for dreamlike, sensual paintings of medieval and literary subjects.
  • His use of intense colour and symbolic beauty helped shape Victorian taste.
  • Influential in developing Symbolism and the Aesthetic Movement.

Ford Madox Brown

Ford Madox Brown, Work 1852–1865
  • Close associate (not a formal Brotherhood member), shared their realism and love of colour.
  • Paintings combined social themes with narrative complexity and authenticity.
  • Mentored and influenced later Pre-Raphaelites and the Arts and Crafts Movement.

Edward Burne-Jones

Edward Burne-Jones, The Beguiling of Merlin 1872–1877
  • Leading artist in the later phase, known for dreamy, decorative paintings and designs.
  • Worked closely with William Morris on stained glass and textiles.
  • His themes were often mythological, with jewel-bright colours and graceful, elongated figures.

William Morris

William Morris, Self-portrait c. 1856
  • Designer, poet, craftsman and central Arts & Crafts reformer.
  • Revived traditional printing, wallpaper, and textile design, inspired by medieval crafts.
  • Opposed mass production—wanted art and design to enrich everyday life.

John Ruskin

John Ruskin, Portrait photo 1863
  • Influential critic and theorist who championed Pre-Raphaelite values.
  • Argued that art should be honest, nature-focused, and express social and moral good.
  • Writings shaped public opinion and supported the movement’s early success.

Elizabeth Siddal

Elizabeth Siddal, Self-portrait c. 1853–1854
  • Painter, poet and inspirational model—muse for many iconic Pre-Raphaelite works (often posed for Millais and Rossetti).
  • Created her own delicate watercolours and drawings echoing medieval themes and melancholy.
  • Life and work highlight the role of women as both subjects and makers in Victorian art.

Three Short Points for Students

  • Pre-Raphaelites painted with sharp detail and bold colours, inspired by nature and medieval stories.
  • They filled their works with hidden meanings from myths, poetry, or legend, using symbolism (like flowers or settings) to add layers of meaning.
  • The group rebelled against the official art world and new industrial society, seeking beauty, sincerity, and a spiritual vision of the past.

Glossary for Students

  • Naturalism: Painting things as they truly appear, with careful attention to detail.
  • Symbolism: Using an object, colour, or scene to represent an idea or emotion.
  • Academic art: Traditional art taught by official academies, often following strict rules.
  • Medievalism: Fascination with the Middle Ages, its stories, art, and style.
