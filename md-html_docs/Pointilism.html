Pointillism

Key Ideas

Painting with Dots of Colour

  • Pointillism is a technique where artists use tiny, separate dots of pure, unmixed colour to create an image.
  • Rather than mixing pigments on a palette, artists place small dots side by side, allowing the viewer’s eye to blend the colours from a distance.
  • This creates a vibrant, shimmering effect and relies on the science of optics—how we see and process colour.

Science and Optical Blending

  • Pointillists were fascinated by colour theory and the science of how we perceive light and shade.
  • They applied paint precisely, using careful planning so the image blends and glows when seen from afar.
  • This technique is more systematic and scientific than Impressionism, aiming for harmony and brilliance by letting your eyes do the mixing.

Patience and New Ways of Seeing

  • Pointillism requires patience and precision—every dot matters.
  • The focus was on the painting method rather than the subject matter itself.
  • The technique encourages viewers to step back and see the whole picture, changing how people think about painting and perception.

Context

Main Locations

  • Paris, France (the movement's centre)
  • Belgium, Holland, and eventually other European cities

Relevant Previous Art Movements

  • Impressionism – Used dabs of paint for light, but Pointillism went further with dots and scientific planning.
  • Neo-Impressionism – Pointillism is a branch of this movement.

Cultural and Political Background

  • Late 19th-century Europe: an era of scientific discovery, industrial growth, and fascination with progress and technology.
  • Artists were inspired by breakthroughs in science, especially optics (the study of light and vision).

Inventions and Technology

  • Advances in colour chemistry and new paint pigments
  • Early photography and scientific research on vision
  • The pixel effect in Pointillism is similar to how digital screens display images, using many tiny coloured dots.

Fashion

  • Men: Three-piece suits, bowler hats, moustaches
  • Women: High-necked blouses, long skirts, corsets, and decorative hats

Period

  • 1880–1890 (core years, though some continued the technique after)

Key Artists

Georges Seurat

Georges Seurat, A Sunday Afternoon on the Island of La Grande Jatte 1884–1886
  • French painter who invented Pointillism (also known as Divisionism).
  • Built images from countless dots of unmixed colour, which visually blend when seen from a distance.
  • A Sunday Afternoon on the Island of La Grande Jatte is a famous example and a manifesto for the style.
  • Influenced by scientific theories about colour and vision; his innovations paved the way for Neo-Impressionism.

Paul Signac

Paul Signac, Grand Canal (Venice) 1905
  • Close collaborator of Seurat and the movement's leader after Seurat's death.
  • Produced vibrant landscapes, seascapes, and urban views using dots and strokes of colour.
  • Promoted Pointillism across Europe through his writing and leadership in the Société des Artistes Indépendants.

Camille Pissarro

Camille Pissarro, Le Pont Boieldieu à Rouen, temps gris 1896
  • Impressionist elder who briefly adopted Pointillism in the 1880s after meeting Seurat and Signac.
  • Applied the technique to landscapes and rural scenes, experimenting with optical colour mixing.
  • This phase contributed to the wider acceptance of Neo-Impressionism.
