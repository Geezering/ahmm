Impressionism (1870–1880)

Overview

Impressionism was an innovative art movement in late 19th-century France, characterised by fresh approaches to light, colour, and everyday life. The movement broke with the academic traditions of detailed, historical painting, instead focusing on capturing immediate impressions of modern scenes.

Key Ideas

Light, Colour, and the Moment

  • Artists like Claude Monet, Berthe Morisot, and Pierre-Auguste Renoir focused on painting the changeable effects of light and colour as they appeared in a specific moment.
  • Visible, quick brushstrokes and painting outdoors (en plein air) helped them show shifting sunlight, shadows, and reflections.
  • The goal was to convey an impression of the scene, not a perfect or thoroughly detailed image.

Modern Life and Subjects

  • Impressionists chose ordinary, everyday subjects: city streets, train stations, gardens, and people enjoying leisure.
  • Edgar Degas and Mary Cassatt depicted daily life with unusual viewpoints, as if glimpsed in passing.
  • Their art felt fresh and spontaneous, closely connected to the world around them.

Innovative Techniques

  • Impressionists used loose, broken brushwork, bright colours, and open compositions, letting objects blend into their surroundings.
  • They rejected smooth, traditional finishes, making their work appear sketchy or unfinished to critics.
  • New painting technology (like paint tubes) and ideas about colour helped them work more freely outdoors.

Context

Main Locations

  • Paris, especially the city and surrounding countryside

Previous Art Movements

  • Realism – Focused on real life with careful detail
  • Romanticism – Explored emotion and drama, but less of modern, everyday life

Culture and Politics

  • Growth of the middle class and city life in 19th-century France
  • The Industrial Revolution: new trains, urban planning, and leisure activities
  • Changing roles for women and new ways of seeing the world

Inventions and Technology

  • Paint in tubes (allowed painting outdoors)
  • Portable easels
  • Photography (inspired capturing fleeting moments and unusual angles)

Fashion

  • Men: Suits with waistcoats, bowler hats, walking sticks
  • Women: Bustled skirts, fitted bodices, decorated hats

Period

  • Main period: 1870–1880
  • Wider influence: 1870–1920

Key Artists

Claude Monet

Claude Monet, Impression, Sunrise 1872
  • Leader of Impressionism, known for vivid landscapes, gardens, and water scenes
  • Captured changing light and atmosphere with quick, loose brushwork
  • Series paintings (“Water Lilies”, “Haystacks”) explored the same view in different conditions

Alfred Sisley

Alfred Sisley, The Bridge at Moret-sur-Loing 1893
  • Specialised in tranquil landscapes and river scenes, often outdoors
  • Used soft, cool colours and delicate handling of light
  • Favoured subtle harmony and natural observation over drama

Edgar Degas

Edgar Degas, The Ballet Class c. 1874
  • Focused on scenes of city life, dancers, and modern interiors
  • Used bold compositions and captured fleeting movement
  • Expert in drawing and pastels, brought a psychological edge to everyday moments

Camille Pissarro

Camille Pissarro, Boulevard Montmartre at Night 1897
  • Key group organiser and mentor, supporter of others in the movement
  • Painted rural labour, village scenes, and changing seasons
  • Constantly evolved his style, remaining committed to painting from direct observation

Pierre-Auguste Renoir

Pierre-Auguste Renoir, Dance at Le Moulin de la Galette 1876
  • Celebrated for joyful scenes of social gatherings and leisure
  • Used luminous colours and lively brushwork, especially in skin and fabrics
  • Later developed a more classical style, but key to the initial movement

Berthe Morisot

Berthe Morisot, The Cradle 1872
  • Pioneered the role of women in Impressionism
  • Painted intimate domestic scenes, women, and children
  • Brought a fresh perspective with swift, expressive brushwork and sensitivity to light

Mary Cassatt

Mary Cassatt, The Child's Bath 1893
  • American artist active in Paris, painted mothers and children
  • Used pastel and modern compositions to capture private, emotional moments
  • Promoted Impressionism in America and championed women in art

Gustave Caillebotte

Gustave Caillebotte, Paris Street; Rainy Day 1877
  • Painted modern Parisian streets, interiors, and effects of rain and light
  • Blended Impressionist colour with detailed, photographic realism
  • Brought a unique urban vision to the movement

Related Figures

Writers

  • Stéphane Mallarmé
  • Émile Zola – Supported the Impressionists and wrote about modern life

Composers

  • Claude Debussy
  • Maurice Ravel – Created musical “impressions” of mood and atmosphere

Glossary

  • En plein air: Painting outdoors, directly from the scene
  • Brushstroke: The visible mark made by a brush, often lively in Impressionist works
  • Composition: The arrangement of elements in a painting
  • Academic painting: Traditional, smooth-finished art taught in schools, following strict rules
