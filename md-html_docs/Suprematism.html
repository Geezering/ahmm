Suprematism

Pure Abstraction and Geometric Forms

  • Suprematism is an art movement based on pure abstraction—art that doesn’t represent real objects or people.
  • Artists used basic geometric shapes like squares, circles, and rectangles, often painted in bold, flat colours.
  • This is called non-objective art: it has no direct link to the real world—just shapes and colours for their own sake.

Supremacy of Feeling and Spirituality

  • Kazimir Malevich, the movement’s founder, argued that art should express pure artistic feeling, not copy the material world.
  • Suprematist works were meant to connect with viewers spiritually, using simplicity and harmony to evoke deep emotion and a sense of higher meaning.

Minimalism and the “Zero Point” of Art

  • Suprematists reduced art to essentials, often using a limited colour palette (black, white, red, and primary colours) and avoiding any depth or storytelling.
  • Malevich described this as reaching the “zero degree” of painting—beginning anew, without references to the past, just pure form and colour.

Contextual Information

Main Locations

  • Russia, especially Moscow and Petrograd (now St Petersburg)

Relevant Previous Art Movements

  • Russian Futurism (emphasised speed and technology)
  • Analytical Cubism (breaking forms into geometric shapes)
  • Impressionism and Post-Impressionism (earlier abstraction experiments)

Relevant Culture and Politics

  • Early 20th-century Russia—a time of revolution and rapid social change
  • Artists aimed to break from the past and reflect the radical spirit of the Russian Revolution, creating art for a new society
  • Suprematism was less political than Constructivism, but still revolutionary in its rejection of tradition

Contemporary Inventions and Technology

  • Rapid growth of trains, electricity, and radio
  • New materials: synthetic paints
  • Early experiments with photography and film

Typical Fashions of the Day

  • Men: Suits with high collars, waistcoats, and top hats or bowlers
  • Women: Long skirts, high-necked blouses, moving later toward simpler modern dresses
  • Avant-garde circles: Some artists wore simple, practical clothing to show their break with tradition

Artists and Period

  • Main period: 1913–1925
  • Build up and legacy: 1910–1925

Kazimir Malevich

Kazimir Malevich, Black Square 1915
  • Founder of Suprematism, focused on geometric forms and limited colours to express pure artistic feeling.
  • His “Black Square” marks a radical break from representational art and the pursuit of spiritual transcendence through abstraction.
  • His theories and works had a profound influence on Russian and global modern art, championing art’s autonomy from the material world.

El Lissitzky

El Lissitzky, Proun VII 1917
  • Pivotal figure in the Russian avant-garde, working in painting, graphic design, architecture, and exhibition design.
  • Developed the concept of “Proun”—dynamic geometric compositions bridging painting and architecture.
  • His work was deeply political, aiming to merge art with technology and promote social change in the new Soviet state.

Alexander Rodchenko

Alexander Rodchenko, Non-Objective Painting 1918
  • Leading Constructivist; active in painting, photography, sculpture, and graphic design with a focus on social engagement.
  • Pioneered photomontage and new photographic perspectives, reflecting the modern industrial age.
  • Emphasised functionalism and collectivism; shaped the course of Soviet art and visual communication.

Lyubov Popova

Lyubov Popova, A Painterly Architectonic 1915
  • Painter and designer associated with Cubo-Futurism and Constructivism, known for bold geometric abstraction and dynamic compositions.
  • Worked in painting, stage design, textiles, and graphic art, committed to integrating art into daily life and industry.
  • Pioneered experimental, collective creativity in the Russian avant-garde.

Aleksandra Ekster

Aleksandra Ekster, Construction 1922
  • Artist acclaimed for vibrant, theatrical designs synthesising Cubism, Futurism, and Constructivism.
  • Excelled in stage and costume design with bold colour, movement, and innovative forms.
  • Influential teacher, spreading avant-garde principles in Russia and Europe, impacting modernist theatre and design.

Olga Rozanova

Olga Rozanova, Green Stripe 1917
  • Abstract artist known for expressive use of colour and non-objective compositions within Suprematism.
  • Paintings feature rhythmic, bold colour planes for pure visual sensation and poetic abstraction.
  • Her innovative work and theoretical writings advanced Russian abstract art.

Nadezhda Udaltsova

Nadezhda Udaltsova, Non-Objective Composition 1916
  • Key member of the Russian avant-garde, active in Cubo-Futurist and Suprematist circles.
  • Work marked by energetic brushwork, fragmented forms, and vibrant colours, exploring boundaries between abstraction and representation.
  • Pioneering teacher and advocate for women in the arts, shaping early Soviet artistic education.

Ivan Kliun

Ivan Kliun, Spheric Suprematism 1922
  • Major Russian avant-garde artist, closely linked to Suprematism and later Constructivism.
  • Evolved from Symbolism and Cubism to pure geometric abstraction, focusing on both spiritual and scientific aspects.
  • Theoretical contributions and experimental works shaped early Russian modernist art.

Three Short Points for Students

  • Suprematism uses geometric shapes and pure colours to create totally abstract art, with no link to the real world.
  • The movement aimed to express pure feeling and spirituality—art as a way to connect to something higher than everyday life.
  • Suprematists removed all detail, focusing on minimalism and the “zero point” of painting—a radical break from tradition.

Glossary for Students

  • Abstraction: Art that doesn’t try to look like real things.
  • Non-objective art: Art with no reference to the real world—just shapes, colours, and lines.
  • Minimalism: Using the simplest and fewest elements possible.
  • Zero point: Starting from scratch, with no reference to past art.
