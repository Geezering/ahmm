New Objectivity

Key Themes, Ideas, and Artistic Aspects

Unsentimental Realism and Social Critique

  • New Objectivity artists rejected Expressionist emotion, choosing clear, sharp realism.
  • Their art depicted everyday life and the harsh realities of post-World War I Germany—poverty, corruption, and social struggle.
  • Works were often bleak, satirical, and critical, exposing injustice and moral decay.

Detached, Analytical Style

  • Known for a sober, unemotional approach with precise detail and clarity.
  • Favoured static, rigid compositions and cool observation over dramatic effects.
  • Emphasised alienation and cynicism, reflecting the era’s disillusionment.

Documenting Modern Life and Urban Change

  • Portrayed rapid urban development, the metropolis, and alienation in modern society.
  • Subjects included city workers, war veterans, politicians, prostitutes, and more, capturing Weimar Republic life’s instability and complexity.

Contextual Information

Main Locations

  • Germany, especially Mannheim, Berlin, and other major cities

Relevant Previous Art Movements

  • Expressionism (New Objectivity was a reaction against its emotion and abstraction)
  • Realism (for focus on truthful, detailed depiction)
  • Dada and Cubism (some stylistic influence)

Culture and Politics

  • Post-World War I Germany: economic hardship, political unrest, and the Weimar Republic
  • Widespread disillusionment, urbanisation, and social upheaval
  • Movement ended when the Nazis rose to power and suppressed modern art (1933)

Contemporary Inventions and Technology

  • Photography (inspired the movement’s documentary style)
  • Printing and mass media, early radio and cinema

Typical Fashions of the Day

  • Men: Suits, fedoras or bowler hats, trench coats
  • Women: Flapper dresses, bobbed hair, cloche hats, and more practical, modern clothes reflecting changing roles

Artists and Period

  • Period: 1920–1930 (core years; ended with the rise of Nazism in 1933)

Key Artists

George Grosz

George Grosz, Pillars of Society 1926
  • German painter and caricaturist, famous for sharp social satire and caustic images of Weimar Berlin’s corruption and chaos.
  • Combined precise draftsmanship with grotesque exaggeration to expose urban society’s ills.
  • His art openly criticised militarism and hypocrisy, leading to both acclaim and persecution.

Max Beckmann

Max Beckmann, Self-portrait in Tuxedo 1927
  • Painter and printmaker who bridged Expressionism and New Objectivity, known for dramatic compositions and psychological depth.
  • Paintings feature complex allegories, self-portraits, and urban life, exploring the trauma and upheaval of 20th-century Europe.
  • Work is recognised for strong outlines, intense colours, and existential themes.

Otto Dix

Otto Dix, Stormtroopers advancing under gas 1924
  • Central figure in New Objectivity, best known for unflinching depictions of war, social injustice, and Weimar underworld.
  • Works include shocking images of WWI, as well as portraits of prostitutes and war veterans in stark detail.
  • Condemned as “degenerate” by the Nazis, now hailed for his honesty and skill.

Christian Schad

Christian Schad, Sonja 1928
  • Painter and photographer associated with both Dada and New Objectivity, noted for hyper-realistic portraits of Berlin and Vienna society.
  • Early innovator in abstract photography, making “Schadographs” (photograms made with light-sensitive paper).
  • His portraiture combines psychological insight, clarity, and a cool, ambiguous style.

Albert Renger-Patzsch

Albert Renger-Patzsch, Fagus-Werk 1927
  • Photographer and leading New Objectivity figure, known for sharply focused, unadorned images of plants, machinery, and landscapes.
  • Book “Die Welt ist schön” (“The World is Beautiful”) celebrated the objective beauty of everyday things.
  • Helped shape modernist photography in Germany, stressing clarity and directness.

August Sander

August Sander, Bricklayer 1929
  • Pioneering photographer best known for “People of the 20th Century,” a typological portrait of Germans of all backgrounds.
  • Took a systematic, objective approach, photographing people from all walks of life as a record of the period.
  • His work is famous for honesty, sociological insight, and its influence on later documentary photography.

Three Short Points for Students

  • New Objectivity artists painted the world with sharp realism, exposing problems and injustices of post-war German society.
  • Their style was detached and analytical, with precise detail and clear compositions showing the cynicism of the time.
  • The movement documented the rapid changes of modern urban life, focusing on ordinary people and the struggles of everyday existence.

Relevant Writers and Composers

Writers

  • Bertolt Brecht (playwright and poet, social critic)
  • Alfred Döblin

Composers

  • Kurt Weill (composer of “The Threepenny Opera” and other socially critical music)

Glossary for Students

  • Satirical: Using humour or exaggeration to criticise or mock something
  • Alienation: Feeling isolated or separated from society or others
  • Weimar Republic: The German government from 1919 to 1933, marked by instability and change
  • Detached: Not showing strong emotions; distant or objective
