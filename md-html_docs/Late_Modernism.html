Late Modernism (1960–1970): Key Themes, Ideas, and Artistic Aspects

Three Short Points

  • Late Modernism focused on abstraction: large areas of pure colour, simple shapes, and clear, flat surfaces.
  • Artists aimed to remove personal emotion, using repeated patterns, hard edges, and sometimes industrial techniques for an impersonal look.
  • They explored new materials, techniques, and ways art interacts with gallery space, resulting in objective, visually impactful paintings.

Emphasis on Abstraction, Colour, and Form

  • Artists like Morris Louis, Kenneth Noland, Jules Olitski, Ellsworth Kelly, and Frank Stella focused on large fields of pure colour, simple geometric forms, and flatness.
  • Art removed references to the real world, concentrating on colour and form alone.
  • This style—known as Post-Painterly Abstraction—was clean, precise, and not about the artist’s emotion or gesture.

Reduction of Personal Expression

  • Unlike Abstract Expressionism, Late Modernists sought to avoid displaying personal feelings or stories.
  • They used regular patterns, crisp lines, and sometimes industrial methods to create a sense of objectivity.
  • The focus was on the direct visual experience: “what you see is what you see.”

Exploration of Materials and Space

  • Experimented with acrylic paints, spraying techniques, and shaped canvases, making works with a machine-made appearance.
  • Some works were shaped or large-scale, changing how viewers move and interact with them in a gallery.
  • Clarity, order, and visual impact were prioritised over storytelling or decoration.

Contextual Information

Main Location(s)

  • United States (especially New York), with influence in Western Europe

Relevant Previous Art Movements

  • Abstract Expressionism (dramatic, emotional, gestural painting)
  • Minimalism (simplicity, geometric shapes)
  • Colour Field Painting (large areas of colour)

Relevant Culture and Politics

  • 1960s: Marked by civil rights, student protests, counterculture, social change, and questioning of tradition.
  • The rise of consumer culture and new technology shaped art and society.

Relevant Writers and Composers

Writers

  • Clement Greenberg (art critic, championed abstraction)
  • Donald Judd (artist and writer, known for Minimalism)

Composers

  • Steve Reich
  • Philip Glass (minimalist music—repetition and simplicity)

Contemporary Inventions and Technology

  • Acrylic paints and new synthetic materials for art
  • Mass production, industrial fabrication methods
  • Television and emerging new media

Typical Fashions of the Day (1960s)

  • Men: Slim-cut suits, turtlenecks, simple shirts, and more casual styles
  • Women: Shift dresses, bold geometric patterns, vibrant colours, minimalist accessories
  • Both: Simplicity, clean lines, and a move away from elaborate ornamentation

Artists and Period: 1960–1970

Morris Louis

Morris Louis, Delta Theta 1960
  • Leading figure in Colour Field painting and the Washington Color School.
  • Used soak-stain technique—pouring thinned acrylic paint onto raw canvas for luminous, flowing veils of colour.
  • Emphasised flatness, pure colour, and lack of visible brushwork, reshaping abstract art post-war.

Kenneth Noland

Kenneth Noland, Beginning 1964
  • Pioneered Colour Field painting, famous for targets, chevrons, and stripes in vibrant hues.
  • Used soak-stain, highlighting the relationship of image to canvas edge and flat fields of colour.
  • Shaped canvases and bold geometric compositions advanced American abstraction.

Jules Olitski

Jules Olitski, Inland Sun 1965
  • Key Colour Field painter, known for atmospheric, spray-painted canvases with subtle colour gradations.
  • Used spray guns for soft-edged, glowing layers and created depth through overlapping colour fields.
  • Emphasised sensory and spatial qualities of colour.

Ellsworth Kelly

Ellsworth Kelly, Orange Red Relief 1990
  • Hard-edged, minimalist painting with bold, clear colours and simple geometric shapes, often on shaped canvases.
  • Focussed on direct visual and spatial relationships between form and colour—no illusionism, just pure presence.
  • Influenced both Colour Field and Minimalism for clarity and precision.

Frank Stella

Frank Stella, Harran II 1967
  • Pioneered shaped canvases and geometric, hard-edged patterns that rejected expressive brushwork.
  • Early “Black Paintings” and later vividly coloured works used repetition, symmetry, and emphasised the object quality of the painting.
  • Famous for saying, "What you see is what you see"—his motto for non-representational art that led to Minimalism and Colour Field painting.

Glossary for Students

  • Abstraction: Art that does not show real things but uses shapes, colours, and lines for visual effect
  • Post-Painterly Abstraction: A style of clean, flat, non-emotional painting, often with hard edges and pure colour
  • Minimalism: Art style using the simplest forms and no extra decoration
  • Hard edge: A sharp, clear boundary between areas of colour or shape
