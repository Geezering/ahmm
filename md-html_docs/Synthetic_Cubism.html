Synthetic Cubism

Key Themes, Ideas, and Artistic Aspects

Collage and Mixed Media

  • Introduced collage (papier collé), using real-world materials such as newspaper, wallpaper, and fabric in artworks.
  • Blended painting with everyday objects, encouraging viewers to consider both the materials and the meaning of the artwork.

Simplified Shapes and Bright Colours

  • Used simpler, clearer shapes and bolder, brighter colours than earlier Analytical Cubism.
  • Subjects were easier to recognise; artists often played with flatness and surface rather than trying to create depth.

Blurring Reality and Art

  • Combined real objects with painted elements, creating a “synthesis” of reality and imagination.
  • Challenged the difference between high art and everyday life, often using playful or symbolic references to popular culture and politics.

Contextual Information

Main Locations

  • Paris (the centre); also artists from Spain, Russia, Britain, the USA, and Mexico contributed.

Relevant Previous Art Movements

  • Analytical Cubism Broke down objects into complex shapes and multiple viewpoints.
  • Post-Impressionism Influenced by use of bold colour and structure.
  • Cézanne’s work: Inspired Cubist ideas about form and perspective.

Relevant Culture and Politics

  • Early 20th-century Europe: Rapid change, new inventions, social upheaval before and after World War I.
  • Artists responded to urbanisation, modern life, and the rise of mass media.

Contemporary Inventions and Technology

  • Photography and early cinema
  • Printing technology (newspapers, magazines, posters)
  • New pigments and artist materials

Typical Fashions of the Day

  • Men: Tailored suits, bowler hats, waistcoats, short hair
  • Women: Straight, loose dresses (towards the “flapper” style), bobbed hair, cloche hats

Period

  • 1912–1919 (main years for Synthetic Cubism)

Key Artists

Pablo Picasso

Pablo Picasso, Still Life with Chair Caning 1912
  • Spanish artist and co-founder of Cubism with Georges Braque.
  • Pioneered collage and reinvented his style through multiple phases, including Analytical and Synthetic Cubism.
  • Revolutionised art with iconic works and relentless innovation, shaping the direction of Western art.

Georges Braque

Georges Braque, Fruit Dish and Glass 1912
  • French painter and collagist; developed Cubism with Picasso.
  • Known for pioneering collage (papier collé) and for still life and landscape subjects.
  • Braque's innovations in collage helped drive the shift to Synthetic Cubism.

Juan Gris

Juan Gris, The Sunblind 1914
  • Spanish painter known for precise, geometric compositions and harmonious colour.
  • Brought structure and clarity to Cubism, often using Synthetic Cubist elements.
  • His style and theoretical writings contributed to Cubism's intellectual foundation.

Fernand Léger

Fernand Léger, Still Life with a Bottle 1914
  • French painter who fused Cubism with themes of modernity and machinery.
  • Known for bold colours, cylindrical forms, and simple, dynamic compositions.
  • Bridged Cubism with the Machine Age, influencing later movements like Purism and Pop Art.

Jean Metzinger

Jean Metzinger, The Blue Bird 1913
  • French painter, theorist, and co-author of the first major text on Cubism, “Du Cubisme.”
  • Works often have fragmented forms and multiple viewpoints.
  • His intellectual approach shaped the understanding of Cubist art.

Roger de La Fresnaye

Roger de La Fresnaye, The Conquest of the Air 1913
  • French painter noted for vibrant colour and lyrical, structured compositions.
  • Brought a poetic, human quality to Cubist art through scenes of modern life and leisure.

Alexander Archipenko

Alexander Archipenko, Walking Woman 1912
  • Ukrainian-born sculptor, took Cubist ideas into sculpture using geometric forms and negative space.
  • Used both traditional and new materials in innovative ways, challenging ideas about volume and mass.
  • Helped integrate Cubism into modern sculpture.

Stuart Davis

Stuart Davis, Lucky Strike 1921
  • American artist known for jazz-inspired, vibrant Cubist compositions of urban scenes.
  • Works feature bold colours, dynamic shapes, and rhythmic movement.
  • Precursor to American modernism and Pop Art.

Ben Nicholson

Ben Nicholson, 1932 (still life) 1932
  • British artist influenced by Cubism; known for abstract, geometric compositions with relief features.
  • His work helped shape modern British art, blending balance and harmonious space.

Diego Rivera

Diego Rivera, Zapatista Landscape 1915
  • Mexican painter who combined Cubism with Mexican themes before becoming known for large, socially focused murals.
  • Cubist phase marked by bold structure and vibrant colour.

František Kupka

František Kupka, Amorpha 1912
  • Czech painter, pioneer of abstract and Orphic Cubism.
  • Explored colour, movement, and music in dynamic, abstract compositions.
  • Theoretical writings and paintings pushed Cubism towards pure abstraction.

Three Short Points for Students

  • Synthetic Cubism introduced collage, mixing paint with real objects like newspaper and fabric to create playful, multi-layered artworks.
  • The movement used simple shapes and bright colours, making pictures that were easier to recognise than earlier Cubist art.
  • Artists blurred the line between art and everyday life, challenging traditional ideas and making viewers think about what art could be.

Related Writers and Composers

Writers

  • Guillaume Apollinaire: Poet and key Cubism supporter.

Composers

  • Igor Stravinsky: Modern, experimental music matched Cubist ideas.
  • Erik Satie

Glossary for Students

  • Collage: Artwork made by sticking different materials (like paper or fabric) onto a surface.
  • Figurative: Art that shows real things, especially people or objects.
  • Perspective: The technique of showing depth or distance in a picture.
  • Synthesis: Combining different elements to make something new.
