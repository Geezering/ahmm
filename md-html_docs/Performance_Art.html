Performance Art

Overview

Performance Art is a form of contemporary art where the artist’s own body, actions, and presence become the artwork itself. Instead of creating permanent objects, performance artists focus on live, real-time experiences. Works are often engaging, challenging, and can be emotional or confrontational.

Key Themes and Ideas

The Body and Direct Experience

  • The artist’s body is central—performance art is live, often unrepeatable, and uses actions instead of traditional art objects.
  • Artists like Marina Abramović and Joseph Beuys explored endurance, presence, and vulnerability through their own bodies.
  • Viewers experience the work in real time, making each performance unique and often intense or emotionally charged.

Breaking Boundaries and Audience Involvement

  • Performance Art dissolves the boundary between art and daily life—performances may occur in galleries, on the street, or in unexpected places.
  • Audience participation is often encouraged; works like Yoko Ono’s "Cut Piece" directly involve viewers in the art.
  • Performances can be unpredictable, provocative, and may challenge or question social norms and the definition of art itself.

Social, Political, and Feminist Messages

  • Artists use performance to address civil rights, feminism, war, and identity.
  • Feminist artists such as Carolee Schneemann and Linda Mary Montano challenged stereotypes and re-examined the female body in art.
  • Many performances respond to current events, aiming to provoke thought and, at times, inspire change.

Context and Background

Main Locations

  • New York, USA (especially SoHo and avant-garde galleries)
  • Western Europe (notably Germany, Austria, France, UK)

Influences and Earlier Movements

  • Dada (anti-art, spontaneity, and chance)
  • Futurism (confrontational, energetic spectacles)
  • Fluxus (experimental, participative happenings)
  • Conceptual and Installation Art (ideas and viewer experience)

Culture and Society (1960s–70s)

  • Political unrest: civil rights, feminism, anti-war protests, Vietnam War
  • Social change, questioning of tradition and authority

Technology and Inventions

  • Portable cameras and video for documenting performances
  • Television and mass media spreading avant-garde ideas
  • New sound/lighting technologies enabled more complex live events

Typical Fashion

  • Men: Jeans, turtlenecks, casual jackets, or suits for formal events
  • Women: Trousers, mini-skirts, loose tops, expressive and individual styles
  • Both: Looks reflected the counterculture—comfortable, creative, unconventional

Relevant Writers and Composers

  • Writers: Antonin Artaud (theatre theorist), Allan Kaprow ("happenings"), Susan Sontag
  • Composers: John Cage (chance, experimentation), Laurie Anderson (performance and sound), Fluxus musicians

Artists and Period

  • Core period: 1965–1975 (many continued afterwards)

Key Artists

Yves Klein

Yves Klein,  Anthropométrie de l’époque bleue (ANT 82)  1960
  • Pioneered the use of “International Klein Blue” in monochrome painting and performance.
  • Created "Anthropometries"—paintings made with human bodies as “living brushes.”
  • Staged radical events, such as selling empty space as art and leaping into the void.
  • Sought to make his art immaterial—focusing on performance, colour, and concept over physical objects.
  • Influenced minimal, conceptual, and later performance art.

Joseph Beuys

Joseph Beuys,  How to Explain Pictures to a Dead Hare  1965
  • Used performance, lectures, and installations to promote art as a means of social transformation.
  • Used symbolic materials (felt, fat) and mythic narratives in performances such as "How to Explain Pictures to a Dead Hare."
  • Originated the idea of "social sculpture": believing everyone has creative potential to shape society.
  • Emphasised healing, participation, and activism in art.
  • Major influence on postwar performance and conceptual art.

Linda Mary Montano

Linda Mary Montano,  Performance portrait  1987
  • Pioneering American artist known for long-duration performances exploring endurance and personal ritual.
  • Lived tied to another artist for a year; experimented with years of imposed silence and other transformative actions.
  • Work fuses the spiritual, autobiographical, and physical, often blurring art and ordinary life.
  • Key influence on feminist and body art practices.

Vito Acconci

Vito Acconci,  Seedbed  1972
  • Explored the body, language, and space through provocative and sometimes unsettling live actions.
  • Famous for performances like "Seedbed," involving physically and psychologically intense actions in public spaces.
  • Later moved into architecture and public art, but retained a focus on audience engagement and interactivity.

Marina Abramović

Marina Abramović,  The Artist Is Present  2010
  • Internationally acclaimed for physically and emotionally demanding performances.
  • Works like “Rhythm 0” and “The Artist Is Present” test endurance, vulnerability, and the boundary between performer and audience.
  • Helped define performance art’s importance in the late 20th and early 21st centuries.
  • Her legacy is tied to exploring presence, trust, and the human condition.

Martha Wilson

Martha Wilson,  Performance portrait  c. 1978
  • Founder of Franklin Furnace and key figure in alternative, experimental, and feminist art scenes.
  • Uses role-play, parody, and her own body to satirise ideas of gender, identity, and power.
  • Champion for feminist performance and supporting emerging artists.

Carolee Schneemann

Carolee Schneemann,  Meat Joy  1964
  • Expanded the possibilities of body art and feminist performance through taboo-defying works.
  • Famous for visceral performances such as "Meat Joy" and "Interior Scroll" exploring sexuality, gender, and self-expression.
  • Influential across performance, video, and installation art.

Yoko Ono

Yoko Ono,  Cut Piece  1964
  • Key figure in Fluxus, performance, conceptual art, and activism.
  • Iconic works (e.g. “Cut Piece”) invited audience participation and explored vulnerability, peace, and collective action.
  • Blurs boundaries between music, visual art, and performance; emphasises art as a catalyst for social change.

Cindy Sherman (early performances)

Cindy Sherman,  Untitled Film Still #21  1978
  • Used her own body to create multiple personas, exploring how identity is constructed and portrayed, especially for women.
  • Early performances and photos often used disguise and role-play to challenge ideas of authenticity and authorship.
  • Helped shape both conceptual and feminist art through her performative approach to photography and identity.

Three Short Points for Students

  • Performance Art makes the artist’s actions, body, and presence into the “artwork”—it’s about experience, not just objects.
  • Works break down barriers between art and life, often through risk, emotion, and involving the audience.
  • The movement engages with social and political themes, especially protest and feminism, using live action to challenge and inspire.

Glossary for Students

  • Ephemeral: Lasting only a short time; not permanent
  • Participatory: Audience is involved in making or shaping the work
  • Endurance: Testing or stretching the limits of the body or mind
  • Counterculture: Alternative beliefs and lifestyles opposed to the social norm
