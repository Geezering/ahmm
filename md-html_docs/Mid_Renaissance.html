Mid Renaissance

During the Mid Renaissance...

  • Artists artists revolutionised painting by making their work look convincing, three-dimensional, and full of life.
  • Artists aimed for realism using depth, shadow, and accurate anatomy.
  • Techniques like linear perspective, chiaroscuro (light and shadow), and close observation transformed art.
  • The result was art far more lifelike than the flat, symbolic styles of the Middle Ages.

Humanism and Classical Influence

  • Inspired by ancient Greece and Rome, artists developed humanism — focusing on individual people, emotions, and achievements.
  • Portraits, myths, and scenes of daily life joined religious stories in their art.
  • Botticelli and others pursued harmony and beauty, celebrating humans as central to the world.

Innovation and Patronage

  • New techniques and materials flourished, supported by wealthy families like the Medici in Florence.
  • Artists gained status as creative thinkers, exploring new ways to represent stories and space.
  • Experiments with fresco, oil, and perspective led to dynamic, detailed backgrounds.
  • Art was now displayed in public buildings, churches, and private homes, spreading Renaissance ideas far and wide.

Contextual Information

Main Locations

  • Florence
  • Venice
  • Other Italian city-states

Previous Art Movements

  • Medieval art: flat, symbolic, and religious
  • Proto-Renaissance: more naturalism introduced by artists such as Giotto

Culture and Politics

  • Rise of independent city-states and families like the Medici supporting the arts
  • A growing interest in science, mathematics, and learning from the classics

Writers and Composers

Writers

  • Dante Alighieri
  • Petrarch
  • Leon Battista Alberti (noted for his art and architecture writing)

Composers

  • Josquin des Prez
  • Giovanni Pierluigi da Palestrina (early Renaissance music)

Inventions and Technology

  • The printing press: enabled quick sharing of new ideas
  • Painters made advances in oil paint, perspective, and anatomical study

Typical Fashions

  • Men: Tunics, hose, and doublets; often richly decorated
  • Women: Long dresses with fitted bodices, ornate sleeves, veils or headdresses

Artists and Period: 1450–1550

Filippino Lippi

Filippino Lippi, Apparition of the Virgin to Saint Bernard 1486
  • Florentine painter highly skilled in oils, tempera, and fresco.
  • Style blended influences from his father and Botticelli: animated figures and rich, warm colours.
  • Completed Masaccio’s Brancacci Chapel frescoes and led major decorative projects.
  • Influenced later artists with inventive and expressive compositions.

Piero della Francesca

Piero della Francesca, The Flagellation of Christ c. 1460
  • Known for peaceful, mathematically precise compositions.
  • Pioneered linear perspective; paintings show harmony and balance.
  • Theoretical writings on perspective and proportion greatly influenced Renaissance art and architecture.

Gentile Bellini

Gentile Bellini, Sultan Mehmed II 1480
  • Key Venetian painter, expert in portraits and grand, detailed scenes.
  • Acted as a cultural ambassador in Constantinople and absorbed Eastern influences.
  • Works are detailed, colourful, and capture life in Venice and beyond.

Andrea del Verrocchio

Andrea del Verrocchio, Equestrian Statue of Bartolomeo Colleoni 1480s
  • Master sculptor and painter who trained Leonardo da Vinci.
  • Blended technical skill with expressive realism in both sculpture and painting.
  • Major influence on Florentine art, especially through his pupils.

Sandro Botticelli

Sandro Botticelli, The Birth of Venus late 1480s
  • Famous for poetic, flowing paintings like “The Birth of Venus” and “Primavera.”
  • Work features delicate lines, elegant figures, and lyrical movement.
  • Combined classical myth with ideals of grace and beauty.
  • One of the Renaissance’s most recognised and influential painters.

Pietro Perugino

Pietro Perugino, The Delivery of the Keys 1481–1482
  • Leading Umbrian painter celebrated for balance and harmonious composition.
  • Painted gentle, serene figures in idealised landscapes.
  • Taught Raphael and helped develop the High Renaissance style.
  • Known for frescoes and altarpieces, including those in the Sistine Chapel.

Domenico Ghirlandaio

Domenico Ghirlandaio, Renunciation of Worldly Goods 1483–1486
  • Prominent Florentine painter known for large, colourful fresco cycles.
  • Blended biblical scenes with portraits of contemporary citizens.
  • Skilled in vivid colour, natural portraiture, and clear composition.
  • Trained Michelangelo and helped bridge the gap to the High Renaissance.

Glossary for Students

  • Linear perspective: A way of drawing that makes things look three-dimensional.
  • Chiaroscuro: Using light and shadow to make things look ‘real’.
  • Humanism: Belief in the importance and value of people and their achievements.
  • Patronage: Support (often money) given by wealthy people to artists.
