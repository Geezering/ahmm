Fauvism (1904–1907)

Overview

Fauvism was a bold, short-lived art movement in early 20th-century France, characterised by wild colours and expressive brushwork. The name “Fauves” means “wild beasts” in French, describing the artists’ untamed and emotional style.

Core Ideas

Bold Colour and Expressive Brushwork

  • Artists like Henri Matisse, André Derain, and Maurice de Vlaminck used pure, vivid colours straight from the tube—trees might be blue, faces green, skies red.
  • Brushstrokes were energetic and rough, creating movement and emotion instead of realism.
  • Colour was used to express feelings, not to imitate nature.

Simplified Forms and Spontaneity

  • Fauves rejected traditional rules about depth, perspective, and shading.
  • Shapes were simplified and often appear flat, with little attempt to show depth or realistic light and shadow.
  • Compositions were spontaneous and impulsive, focused on the artist’s immediate response rather than careful planning.

Rebellion and Individual Expression

  • Fauvism was a radical break from tradition, inspired by Post-Impressionist artists like Van Gogh, Gauguin, and Cézanne.
  • Artists were encouraged to paint what they felt, not just what they saw, making each work personal and expressive.
  • The approach shocked viewers and earned them the nickname “wild beasts” (les fauves) for their untamed style.

Context

Main Locations

  • France, especially Paris and the Mediterranean coast

Relevant Previous Art Movements

  • Post-Impressionism—especially Van Gogh, Gauguin, Cézanne, and Seurat
  • Impressionism—for painting from life, but Fauvism was more expressive and less realistic

Culture and Politics

  • Early 20th-century France: a period of optimism, rapid change, and artistic experiment.
  • Artists responded to new ideas about psychology, the subconscious, and personal expression.

Inventions and Technology

  • Tubes of paint (easier to transport, allowed for painting outdoors and brighter colours)
  • Advances in printing and photography

Fashion

  • Men: Suits, waistcoats, bowler hats, and moustaches (typical early 20th-century style)
  • Women: Long skirts, high-necked blouses, wide-brimmed hats, and elaborate hairstyles

Period

  • 1904–1907 (core years for Fauvism)

Key Artists

Henri Matisse

Henri Matisse, Woman with a Hat 1905
  • Leader of Fauvism, known for bold colours, simplified shapes, and expressive brushwork.
  • Used exaggerated colour to convey emotion, not reality; later worked in innovative cut-outs.
  • Prioritised decorative composition and colour arrangement, creating joyful and energetic art.

Maurice de Vlaminck

Maurice de Vlaminck, The River Seine at Chatou 1906
  • Known for intense, vivid colour inspired by Van Gogh, and impulsive, energetic painting.
  • Paintings marked by a spontaneous, raw response to nature and frequent use of non-naturalistic hues.
  • Defined the wild, untamed spirit of Fauvism.

André Derain

André Derain, Charing Cross Bridge, London 1906
  • Worked closely with Matisse; used unnatural colours and vivid brushstrokes, especially in landscapes and portraits.
  • Balanced wildness with compositional control, for vibrant yet harmonious paintings.
  • Known for decorative use of colour and simplified forms; later moved to more restrained styles.

Georges Braque

Georges Braque, L'Estaque 1906
  • Briefly adopted Fauvism, using intense colour and expressive brushwork.
  • Mainly painted landscapes, focusing on colour as the main tool rather than realism.
  • His Fauvist phase was short—he soon became a pioneer of Cubism.

Raoul Dufy

Raoul Dufy, Regatta at Cowes 1906
  • Drawn to Fauvism’s bright colours and decorative effects; created lively scenes full of light and movement.
  • Work features fluid lines, simplified shapes, and optimistic, joyful colour.
  • Brought playfulness and spontaneity to Fauvism, especially in portrayals of leisure and coastal life.

Kees van Dongen

Kees van Dongen, Corn Poppy 1907
  • Known for striking, bold portraits with exaggerated colours and outlines.
  • Often painted nightlife, performers, and fashionable women in vivid colours.
  • Embodied the wild and expressive energy of Fauvism; paintings are instantly recognisable.

Georges Rouault

Georges Rouault, Le Christ risquant sa vie 1905
  • Combined intense colour with thick black outlines, giving his figures a stained-glass effect.
  • Focused on spiritual and emotional subjects, using colour to show inner feeling and compassion.
  • His works have a unique, sombre mood and deep psychological intensity.

Albert Marquet

Albert Marquet, La Plage de Sainte-Adresse 1906
