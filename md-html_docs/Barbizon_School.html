Barbizon School (c. 1830–1860)

The Barbizon School marked a shift towards natural, honest landscape painting in 19th-century France. Artists in this group aimed to capture real rural life and the beauty of nature without idealisation or strict academic rules, paving the way for Impressionism.

Key Facts & Context

Main Locations

  • The village of Barbizon, beside the Forest of Fontainebleau, France

Relevant Previous Art Movements

  • Romanticism (dramatic, emotional scenes and idealised nature)
  • Influenced by 17th-century French and Dutch landscape painters, and English landscape traditions

Culture & Politics

  • Mid-1800s France faced rapid industrialisation and urbanisation, bringing nostalgia for country life.
  • The movement opposed the French Academy’s strict hierarchy, which favoured historical painting over landscape.

Relevant Writers & Composers

Writers

  • George Sand (local to Barbizon, celebrated rural France)
  • Victor Hugo (Romantic author) who write 'Les Miserables' and 'The Hunchback of Notre Dame'

Composers

  • Hector Berlioz
  • Charles Gounod (inspired by nature in his music)

Inventions & Technology

  • Portable paint tubes allowed artists to paint outdoors (en plein air).
  • Early photography, encouraging artists to pursue realism and direct observation.

Typical Fashions

  • Men: Frock coats, waistcoats, cravats, top hats
  • Women: Full skirts with petticoats, bonnets, fitted bodices

Artists & Main Period

  • Core years: 1830–1860 (some continued later)

Three main artists

Théodore Rousseau

Théodore Rousseau, The Forest in Winter at Sunset 1846–67
  • Painted realistic, atmospheric landscapes highlighting the changing light and mood of the forest.
  • Worked outdoors (en plein air) to capture the direct feeling of nature.
  • Chose ordinary rural scenes over grand, dramatic views.

Jean-François Millet

Jean-François Millet, The Angelus 1857–59
  • Known for gentle, dignified scenes of rural workers and peasants, showing their daily struggles and connection to the land.
  • Used earthy colours and soft brushwork for realism and empathy.
  • Often included social messages, highlighting respect for rural life.

Charles-François Daubigny

Charles-François Daubigny, Banks of the Oise c. 1863
  • Painted rivers and fields with loose, expressive brushwork and focus on light and mood.
  • Emphasised painting quickly—they wanted to capture fleeting effects in nature, often finishing work on the spot.
  • Supported younger artists and influenced the Impressionists.

Three Short Points for Each Artist

Théodore Rousseau

  1. Painted atmospheric, realistic views of forests and countryside.
  2. Captured changing light and mood by working outdoors.
  3. Valued ordinary scenes, not dramatic or historical subjects.

Jean-François Millet

  1. Focused on rural workers—their dignity and daily lives.
  2. Used earthy colours and gentle brushwork.
  3. Highlighted people’s connection to the land.

Charles-François Daubigny

  1. Painted rivers and fields using lively, expressive brushwork.
  2. Captured atmosphere and changing light by painting quickly outdoors.
  3. Influenced many future artists, including the Impressionists.

In Simple Terms for Students

  • The Barbizon School wanted to paint nature and rural life just as they saw it—not as background for myth or legend.
  • They worked outdoors to get the real colours, light, and feeling of the landscape.
  • Ordinary countryside and peaceful village scenes were their favourite subjects, shown in soft, natural colours.
  • This movement rebelled against strict old rules and helped inspire Impressionism.

Why the Barbizon School Matters

  • Made landscapes a serious subject in their own right.
  • Inspired later artists to look at nature and rural life with fresh eyes.
